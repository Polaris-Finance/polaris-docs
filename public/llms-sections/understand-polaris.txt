# Understand Polaris

## Polaris Core Concepts

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/core-concepts
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/core-concepts.md

## The shape of the system

Polaris has one collateral primitive, one family of pAssets, and one stewardship token.

1. **Swap.** ETH enters the **bonding curve** and is issued as **pETH**; the curve captures volatility.
2. **Collateral.** pETH is deposited as the sole collateral for pAsset **branches**.
3. **Borrow.** A user opens a **CDP** in a branch to mint **pUSD** or another pAsset against that pETH.
4. **Stewardship.** Burning pETH in a conversion auction mints **POLAR**, raising the pETH floor price and releasing ETH.

This page is the system map. Each term is defined briefly and links to its full treatment.

## The assets

A synthetic debt asset minted from a Polaris branch through user CDPs (pUSD, pGOLD, pCHF, ...). Each is overcollateralized by pETH and counterparty-free. See [The pAsset Catalog](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/passet-catalog).

The flagship pAsset, pegged to the US dollar, backed by pETH collateral. See [Minting pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting).

ETH deposited into a two-way [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth). It is not debt minted from a CDP; it is the *only* collateral Polaris accepts, has a contract-defined floor, and is yield-bearing.

The protocol's stewardship token. After a fixed distribution at launch, new POLAR can be minted *only* by burning pETH in a [conversion auction](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions). **vePOLAR**, a vote-escrow layer for stewardship rights, is forthcoming.

## Minting & positions

Collateralized Debt Branch: the aggregate protocol branch for one pAsset. A branch contains that pAsset's minting and redemption logic, debt accounting, risk parameters, Stability Pool, and all user CDPs. See [The pAsset Catalog](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/passet-catalog).

Collateralized Debt Position: your individual borrowing position inside a branch. You post pETH collateral and mint the pAsset directly from the protocol, not by borrowing another user's funds. Each CDP has its own collateral, debt, and loan-to-value (LTV). See [Minting pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting).

The Polaris app term for an individual CDP - the same position, by another name. See [Managing Your Trove](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove).

Polaris's factory layer: each branch issues a different pAsset, all sharing the same pETH collateral and bonding curve. See [StablecoinOS: an open catalog](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/passet-catalog#stablecoinos-an-open-catalog).

## Keeping the peg

Exchanging a pAsset for its underlying collateral at face value (minus a fee) when it trades *below* peg. Restores the floor. See [Redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations).

The inverse of redemption. Arbitrageurs add pETH to troves to mint pAssets when the pAsset trades *above* peg. Restores the ceiling.

Redemption and minting volume serve as onchain proxy signals for the peg, letting Polaris set rates without an external oracle.

## Risk states

If a trove's LTV exceeds the maximum, it is liquidated, and the [Stability Pool](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield) absorbs the debt - or, if the pool is too small, the debt and collateral redistribute to other troves. See [Liquidations](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations).

A protocol-wide protective state entered when the system-wide reserve-to-debt ratio (the [TCR, Total Collateralization Ratio](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources#t)) falls below a fixed threshold. See [Recovery Mode](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode).

The mathematically enforced minimum price of pETH (in ETH), and that floor as a percentage of market price (your maximum drawdown). See [The Floor Price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price).

## Yield & coordination

A reserve of pAssets that processes liquidations and is the primary venue for earning yield. See [The Stability Pool](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield).

The mechanism that mints POLAR by burning pETH. See [Conversion Auctions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions).

Buffers protocol revenue in pETH and streams it to troves pro-rata to debt (Flow recipients forthcoming); POLAR stakers are paid separately from borrower interest. Swap fees are first burned at the floor - raising it - and the released ETH is recycled into pETH. See [The Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router).

A stewardship role steers *quantitative parameters*, with safety-critical bounds hardcoded in the contracts and the remaining bounds set at audited deployment. It does not govern the immutable core. See [Stewardship, not Governance](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship).

## Issuing Polaris pAssets

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting.md

Polaris lets users issue **pUSD**, **pGOLD**, and other pAssets directly from the protocol. You deposit **pETH** as collateral, choose the pAsset and amount you want to issue, and manage the position around its loan-to-value (LTV).

Unlike a money market, Polaris is not matching you with a lender. The protocol issues the pAsset when you open the position, records the debt against your pETH collateral, and lets you repay later to close or reduce that debt.

## Before you issue

- **Collateral:** pETH is the only collateral accepted for pAsset positions. You can acquire it through the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) before opening a position.
- **pAsset:** Public Testnet 1 supports pUSD and pGOLD. Future pAssets are listed in the [pAsset Catalog](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/passet-catalog) only when their status is clear.
- **LTV:** The app shows your projected LTV before you sign. Higher LTV gives you less room before liquidation.
- **Liquidation price:** Your position depends on ETH and pETH collateral value. If collateral value falls far enough, the position can be liquidated automatically.
- **Interest:** Borrow interest accrues to your debt over time, raising LTV if you do nothing.
- **Redemptions:** Redemptions can reduce collateral and debt across open positions even while they remain solvent.

## How issuing works

1. Deposit ETH into the bonding curve to receive pETH, or use pETH you already hold.
2. Deposit pETH as collateral in the app's **Borrow** flow.
3. Choose the pAsset and amount to issue.
4. Review LTV, liquidation buffer, interest, fees, and expected wallet changes.
5. Sign the transaction. The issued pAsset arrives in your wallet, and the debt is recorded against your position.

## What changes after opening

Your position is not static. Several values can move after you issue a pAsset:

- **ETH and pETH pricing** can move your liquidation price and LTV.
- **Interest accrual** increases the debt you owe.
- **pETH yield credited to positions** can increase collateral value over time, partly offsetting borrow costs.
- **System activity** can update recorded balances as issuance, redemptions, liquidations, and Stability Pool flows settle through the protocol.

Use [Manage a Borrow Position](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove) for the operational details: adding collateral, issuing more, repaying debt, closing the position, and keeping enough LTV buffer.

## Risk checks

Before signing, check:

- The selected pAsset and amount.
- The pETH collateral amount.
- The projected LTV and liquidation price.
- The current minimum collateral ratio, max LTV equivalent, and Recovery Mode status on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).
- The borrow interest rate and any fees.
- Whether you will still hold enough pAsset later to repay or close.

Do not treat low LTV as protection from every protocol action. A larger buffer helps you avoid liquidation, but redemptions can still affect open positions. See [Redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations) and [Liquidations](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations).

## Managing a Polaris Trove

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove.md

A trove is a living pAsset debt position. After you [open it](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/open-a-trove), you can adjust it freely: add collateral, issue more, repay debt, or close it. The app-facing health metric is **loan-to-value (LTV)**.

**Scope:** this page covers individual pAsset positions shown under **Borrow**, not reserve loans against fpETH. For definitions, see the [Glossary](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources).

## Loan-to-value (LTV)

Your **loan-to-value (LTV)** is the recorded value of your debt divided by the value of your trove's collateral:

> LTV = debt face value ÷ collateral value

Collateral value comes from the protocol's collateral pricing path. Debt value is what you owe in that pAsset's reference unit, including accrued interest; it is not the temporary market price at which you might buy the pAsset on a DEX. A trove holding pETH worth \$15,000 against 10,000 pUSD of debt has an LTV of 66.7%. Lower is safer.

Some protocol materials and parameters use **ICR** (individual collateral ratio — your trove's current ratio, the reciprocal of LTV) and **MCR** (minimum collateral ratio — the liquidation threshold):

> ICR = collateral value ÷ debt face value

That means 150% ICR is the same position as 66.7% LTV, and a maximum LTV is the reciprocal of the MCR. Two rules matter:

- Above the **maximum LTV**, the trove can be [liquidated](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations).
- [Redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations) draw collateral from every open trove collectively, pro-rata to its recorded collateral and regardless of LTV. Lower LTV protects against liquidation, not redemption.

Your LTV moves on its own as ETH's price moves — you need not touch the trove for it to drift. Managing the trove is mostly about keeping LTV below the risk line with enough room for volatility.

## Trove Health Simulator

The simulator below defaults to the current Public Testnet 1 MCR of 115%, equivalent to about 87.0% max LTV — not the stricter [Recovery Mode](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode) action MCR (called "Emergency Mode" in the contracts). Always verify the live values on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters) and in the app before signing.

Issuing more or withdrawing collateral raises your LTV. Cross the maximum and your trove is liquidated automatically: your collateral covers the debt plus a liquidation penalty, with no margin call, and only the surplus is returned for you to claim. Redemptions can also reach your position while it is still solvent — there is no LTV exemption — shrinking your collateral and pETH exposure proportionally without your consent. See the Risk Disclosure.

## Values to check

Current MCR, max LTV equivalents, Recovery Mode, fee, and decay values live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). Before changing a trove, check the live app quote and the parameter page for the pAsset you are managing.

## Add collateral

Deposit more pETH into the trove to **lower your LTV** without changing your debt. This is the simplest way to step back from a liquidation threshold during a drawdown. (Need pETH? Mint it on the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth).)

## Issue more / repay debt

- **Issue more.** If your LTV has room, issue more pAsset against the same collateral. This *raises* your LTV, so leave a buffer.
- **Repay debt.** Burn pAsset back into the trove to reduce your debt and *lower* your LTV. Repaying is the other lever for recovering a thinning position, and it frees headroom to withdraw collateral.

## Monitor interest accrual

Borrowers pay interest that accrues continuously against the trove. Interest adds to your debt over time, so your LTV drifts *up* even if ETH holds flat and you do nothing. Check your accrued debt periodically and repay or top up so accrual does not quietly walk you toward a threshold.

Collateral pETH also *earns* yield while it sits in the trove, partly offsetting the interest cost; see [Issuing pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting) for the high-level position flow.

### Worked example: interest accrual

Suppose your trove owes 10,000 pUSD and the app quotes an 8% annualized borrow rate. As a rough estimate, before rate changes and compounding:

Period | Interest estimate | New debt

30 days | 10,000 × 8% × 30 / 365 = 65.75 pUSD | 10,065.75 pUSD
90 days | 10,000 × 8% × 90 / 365 = 197.26 pUSD | 10,197.26 pUSD

If your collateral value stays flat at \$15,000, the 30-day estimate moves your LTV from 66.7% to about 67.1%. The contract may accrue continuously and the rate may change, so use the app's recorded debt for precise decisions.

## Drift from system activity

ETH's price and interest accrual are not the only background forces. System-level [issuance and redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations) move every open trove for that pAsset: each issuance adds collateral and debt to open troves pro-rata, each redemption removes both, and pETH yield from the [Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router) credits collateral pro-rata to debt. These changes accrue to your position continuously and are folded into its recorded balances the next time you touch the trove; the position the app shows includes them.

## Transfer the trove

Each trove is an ERC-721 NFT ("Polaris CDP Position"), and whoever holds that NFT controls the position. Transferring it hands over the collateral, the debt, and every lever on this page in one transaction. Standard NFT approvals apply — audit operator approvals as you would for any valuable NFT, and never send a trove to an address you do not control or trust.

## Close the trove

Closing is the reverse of opening (**repay debt ⇒ withdraw collateral**):

1. **Repay the full debt.** Burn enough pAsset to clear the recorded debt, including accrued interest. You must hold (or re-acquire) the pAsset.
2. **Withdraw collateral.** With the debt at zero, withdraw your pETH — including the fixed gas-compensation deposit taken at open. The trove is closed.

If you do not hold enough pAsset to repay (for example, you sold it), buy it back on the market first.

## How LTV maps to your exposure

LTV | Liquidation exposure | Redemption exposure

Low | Minimal — survives large ETH drops | Low — redemptions reduce every trove pro-rata to its collateral, and a large buffer keeps your LTV healthy afterward
Moderate | Survives normal volatility | Moderate — a system-wide redemption shrinks your collateral proportionally and can erode your buffer
Near max | High — a sharp ETH move liquidates you | A system-wide redemption reduces your collateral pro-rata and, on thin margin, can leave you closer to the liquidation line

The takeaway: a buffer is not wasted capital, it is the price of staying in control of your position. Keep enough margin that ordinary volatility and quiet interest accrual cannot force an outcome you did not choose.

pGOLD and the incumbent gold tokens both give gold-price exposure, but they are structurally different products.

Property | pGOLD | XAUT / PAXG

Redemption | Permissionless, no protocol-level minimum published yet | Issuer-gated; account requirements and redemption minimums depend on the issuer's current terms

Counterparty | Polaris protocol only | Tether / Paxos as issuer

Image: pGOLD versus centralized gold tokens (XAUT/PAXG): pGOLD is backed by onchain pETH with permissionless, KYC-free access and Polaris as the only counterparty, while XAUT/PAXG are backed by vaulted bullion with issuer-gated, KYC-required redemption and custody risk

## StablecoinOS: an open catalog

Polaris is **debt-asset agnostic**: it can issue a synthetic pegged to any asset with robust reference pricing and risk parameters. StablecoinOS lets new pAssets share the same pETH collateral pool and bonding curve.

Shared collateral lets teams add a new pAsset without bootstrapping a fresh collateral base. Each pAsset still has its own debt accounting, so stress in one pAsset does not directly liquidate another pAsset's borrow positions.

## pETH Bonding Curve

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth.md

The bonding curve is the foundation of Polaris. From this one primitive come pETH, its rising floor, [Split](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split), reserve loans, and value capture on every interaction.

## What is a bonding curve?

A two-way bonding curve lets you swap like a regular AMM pool, with one key difference: **it needs no liquidity providers.** Instead, it enforces a mathematical relationship between a token's price and its supply:

- Demand rises, tokens are minted, price increases.
- Tokens are burned/sold, price decreases.

Buying adds ETH to the reserve and **mints** pETH. Selling withdraws from the reserve and **burns** pETH. The result is contract-native liquidity with no external liquidity provider, subject to the execution caveats in the [Risk overview](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth#risk-overview) below. You have used the price-curve idea without knowing it: AMMs like Uniswap and Curve run on one (though their liquidity comes from LPs), and Pump.fun is the closest mint/burn example.

Two things make pETH's curve different from Pump.fun's:

1. **Purpose**: Pump.fun raises capital to seed liquidity; Polaris uses the curve to *back* pETH permanently.
2. **Temporality**: Pump.fun's curve terminates at "graduation"; the pETH curve is designed to keep running.

## What's unique about pETH's curve

pETH's curve is **two-way** (ETH ⇒ pETH and pETH ⇒ ETH) and gives pETH a price floor measured in ETH under the curve assumptions. The full floor and floor-ratio explanation lives on [pETH Floor Price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price).

Image: pETH bonding curve: ETH deposits mint pETH along a power-law curve with a rising floor price

*Key takeaway: the curve is both the issuance path and the reserve-backed exit path; fees and conversion burns are designed to lift the floor.*

### The maths

The curve follows the formula:

$$
P = \alpha \cdot Q^{\beta}
$$

- **α**: a scaling parameter of little importance.
- **Q**: the supply of pETH.
- **β**: the power-law exponent that sets the curve's behaviour.

As supply `Q` increases, the price of pETH in ETH increases. **β** sets how tightly pETH tracks ETH: a large β decouples pETH and makes it more volatile; a small β couples them closely. Current values live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

The Public Testnet 1 beta is testnet-only. The production beta remains pending; verify [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters) before using any numeric value.

### How the production curve launches

Production launch is designed to initialize the curve through a dedicated **bootstrap** contract: a time-boxed, whitelisted deposit window with a minimum and maximum ETH raise. Depositors receive pETH when the raise initializes the curve and its starting floor — a portion claimable immediately, the rest vesting over a set period, with a penalized early-exit (rage-quit) option. Public Testnet 1 skipped this phase; its curve was initialized directly. Bootstrap terms and dates are pending on [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status).

## pETH beyond CDPs

pETH is tailor-made collateral for Polaris pAsset branches and user CDPs, but it has a life of its own.

### Reserve loans and Split

You can [split pETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split) into **fpETH** (the floor leg) and **vpETH** (the premium leg). fpETH can collateralize **reserve loans** — WETH borrowed against floor-value, sized to the floor price so premium swings don't liquidate the position. See [pETH Split](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split) for the full mechanism, risks, and merge path.

### DeFi composability

pETH's rising floor and eventual deep ETH backing could make it a prime asset *outside* Polaris too. A pETH/ETH market on Aave, Morpho, or Euler is a natural fit, much like stETH across DeFi today.

## pETH vs stETH

stETH is a prime asset, but it lacks contract-native liquidity from its own reserve. Native withdrawals depend on the validator exit queue and can vary; DEX liquidity is deep, but depends on liquidity providers who can stop supplying in a panic.

Feature | **pETH** | **stETH**

Liquidity | Bonding-curve reserve, subject to contract and chain liveness | Deep, but LP-dependent
Withdrawal | Onchain sell path, with gas/slippage/MEV | Validator-exit queue; timing varies
Price floor | Contract-enforced under audited invariants | None
Main risk | Price risk vs ETH, bounded by the floor under the curve assumptions | LP/liquidity risk

stETH is a major ETH asset, but it does not provide pETH's reserve-backed onchain sell path.

## Risk overview

pETH carries three main risks:

1. **Technical risk**: the bonding curve is a smart contract; technical risk is unavoidable, mitigated through audits and modeling.
2. **Execution risk**: using the curve still requires Ethereum liveness, available gas, and transactions that can tolerate slippage and MEV.
3. **Price risk**: pETH is volatile against ETH. Your exposure depends on your entry price relative to the floor, i.e. the [floor ratio](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price): a higher ratio means a smaller curve-implied drawdown before execution costs.

The curve design removes liquidity-provider withdrawal risk. That does not make execution riskless: the curve provides a contract-defined buy/sell path and floor while the relevant invariants hold.

## pETH Floor Price

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price.md

## What it is

The **floor price** is pETH's mathematically enforced minimum price, in ETH, under the bonding-curve invariants. It is the curve price for the *last person out* if everyone exited the bonding curve at once — assuming the contracts work as designed, Ethereum is live, and users can pay execution costs. Because most exiters get a better curve price, it is a protocol worst case, not an average.

The floor is not a promise or a target defended by a treasury. It is a direct consequence of the bonding curve's reserves and supply, enforced by immutable code and dependent on the audited invariants holding.

## Why it is designed to rise

Two mechanisms push the floor up under the intended curve invariants. Normal protocol actions are not designed to push it down:

- **Swap fees feed the burn.** Every swap on the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) charges a pETH fee that accrues to the protocol's [Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router). Accumulated fee pETH is periodically **burned at the floor price**: supply falls while the reserve releases only the floor value of what was burned, so the floor rises. The released ETH is swapped back into pETH and funds the pETH yield flows.
- **Conversion auctions.** Minting POLAR requires [burning pETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions), which again reduces supply and lifts the floor.

The result is a staircase under normal operation: pETH's market price fluctuates above the floor, while the floor itself steps upward over time.

Image: The rising pETH floor as a staircase stepping up over time, each step driven by a swap-fee burn or a conversion-auction burn that permanently reduces pETH supply

*Each swap-fee burn and conversion auction removes pETH supply, ratcheting the floor up a step — under the curve assumptions.*

## The floor ratio

The **floor ratio** expresses the floor as a percentage of the current market price:

$$
\text{Floor ratio} = \frac{\text{Floor price}}{\text{Market price}} \times 100
$$

It tells you the curve-implied drawdown to the floor before gas, slippage, MEV, and smart-contract or chain-liveness risk. A 90% floor ratio means 10% downside to the floor in ETH terms under those assumptions; 67% means 33% downside.

Floor ratio | Curve-implied drawdown to floor

90% | 10%
67% | 33%
55% | 45%

Image: The floor ratio: a volatile pETH market-price line above a steadily rising floor line; a worked example with market price 1.09 and floor 0.60 gives a 55% floor ratio and a 45% maximum drawdown to the floor

*A higher floor ratio means a smaller curve-implied drawdown to the floor — before gas, slippage, MEV, and technical risk.*

The floor ratio is itself volatile, driven by market sentiment and activity:

- In phases of **wild growth**, the market price runs far ahead of the floor, so the ratio can fall, sometimes below 50%.
- As the market price **normalizes**, the floor catches up and the ratio rises, until the limited remaining downside makes pETH attractive again.

## Assumptions behind the floor

Assumption | Why it matters

Bonding-curve contracts function as audited | The floor is a contract invariant, not an offchain guarantee
Ethereum remains live | Exiting requires a transaction that can be included
Users can pay gas | High gas can make small exits uneconomic
Slippage and MEV are tolerated or bounded | The quoted curve price and realized execution can differ
The relevant invariants stay unchanged and unbroken | The floor depends on reserves, supply accounting, and burn/mint rules

> Your risk in pETH is decided at entry: the spread between your entry price and the floor (the floor ratio), plus execution and technical risk. Under the intended curve invariant, normal protocol activity moves the floor up, not down.

## pETH Split: fpETH and vpETH

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split.md

The **Split** mechanism separates pETH into two matching tokens:

```
1 pETH -> 1 fpETH + 1 vpETH
```

Splitting does not create extra value. It separates the pETH position into a **floor leg** and a **premium leg** so users can hold, trade, merge, or borrow against the part of pETH they actually want.

## The two legs

Token | Meaning | Main use

**fpETH** | Floor-price pETH. It tracks the pETH floor-value claim under the splitter and bonding-curve assumptions. | Hold floor carry, redeem for WETH through the splitter, or use as collateral in reserve loans.
**vpETH** | Volatility-premium pETH. It represents exposure to pETH's market value above the floor. | Hold or trade the premium/upside leg after separating out the floor claim.

Think of pETH as:

```
pETH value = floor value + premium above floor
```

fpETH isolates the floor-value side. vpETH isolates the remaining premium side. If pETH trades close to its floor, vpETH can become worth very little. If pETH trades far above its floor, vpETH carries most of that premium exposure.

For intuition, with pETH at a market price of 1.00 ETH:

Floor price | [Floor ratio](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price) | fpETH claim (floor leg) | vpETH exposure (premium leg)

0.60 ETH | 60% | ≈ 0.60 ETH | ≈ 0.40 ETH
0.90 ETH | 90% | ≈ 0.90 ETH | ≈ 0.10 ETH

The arithmetic is indicative: each leg trades on its own market, so realized prices can deviate from the decomposition.

## Split and merge

**Split** deposits pETH into the splitter contract and mints equal amounts of fpETH and vpETH to your wallet.

**Merge** burns equal amounts of fpETH and vpETH and releases the matching pETH from the splitter.

You need both legs in equal amounts to merge back into pETH. If you sold or deposited one leg, you must reacquire it before merging.

## fpETH redemption

fpETH can be redeemed through the splitter for WETH at the current pETH floor-price value under the contract assumptions. That redemption is the reason fpETH is the **floor leg**.

This is not an offchain guarantee. It depends on the splitter, bonding curve, reserve accounting, Ethereum liveness, gas availability, and the relevant invariants holding.

## Reserve loans

Reserve loans use **fpETH** as collateral, and each loan is recorded onchain as an NFT. The borrowable WETH amount is based on the fpETH amount and current pETH floor price, not on pETH's market premium.

That design reduces price-liquidation risk from pETH premium swings, but it does not make the position risk-free:

- Debt can accrue according to the reserve-loan fee model.
- Closing or adjusting can be affected by floor-price changes before inclusion.
- You still need WETH to repay and unlock fpETH.
- Smart-contract, approval, oracle, gas, and chain-liveness risks remain.

Reserve loans are separate from pAsset troves. A trove mints pUSD, pGOLD, or another pAsset against pETH collateral. A reserve loan borrows WETH against fpETH.

## Floor carry

**Floor carry** is the return a fpETH holder may realize as the pETH floor rises. If the floor price increases while you hold fpETH, the floor-value redemption claim increases under the contract assumptions.

Floor carry is not a fixed APR. It depends on actual floor growth from protocol activity, execution costs, market pricing of fpETH, and whether the redemption path is available when you use it.

## POLAR Conversion Auctions

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions.md

Conversion auctions let converters **burn pETH to mint new POLAR**. This page covers the auction mechanics; POLAR's broader economics live on [POLAR Tokenomics](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/tokenomics).

Image: The conversion mechanism: a converter burns pETH, which mints new POLAR, lifts the pETH floor price, and releases ETH from the bonding-curve reserve; the released ETH splits into a converter rebate and pETH forwarded to the Fee Router

## Spike-and-decay pricing

Conversion runs as a **continuous, permissionless auction**. Anyone can convert pETH to POLAR at any moment; whether it is *profitable* depends on the conversion price versus POLAR's market price. The price follows a **spike-and-decay** model:

1. The conversion price **continuously decays**. The longer nobody converts, the cheaper the offered price — and the more attractive conversion gets.
2. When a conversion happens, the price **spikes upward**, proportional to the fraction of POLAR supply just inflated. Minting 1% of supply spikes the price far more than minting 0.01%.
3. After the spike, decay resumes until the next conversion.

The result is a natural rhythm: the price decays until an arbitrageur sees it drift below POLAR's market price, converts to capture the spread, and spikes it back up — closing the opportunity until decay reopens it.

Image: Spike-and-decay conversion pricing: the conversion price decays continuously until it drops below POLAR's market price, opening an arbitrage window; each conversion spikes the price up by an amount proportional to the fraction of POLAR supply minted, then decay resumes

> **Note:**
> The spike is computed against **POLAR supply, not pETH supply**. This steers conversions toward small, frequent operations: a whale converting a large amount in one go faces rapidly diminishing returns as each increment spikes the price against them. The mechanism self-regulates toward steady, incremental minting.

> *Exact decay and spike parameters are finalized closer to launch, set through stewardship. The converter rebate is bounded between 0% and 100%; final decay and spike bounds are pending.*

## What burning pETH does

Burning pETH through a conversion has two effects beyond minting POLAR:

- **ETH is released from the bonding curve reserve.** The ETH backing the burned pETH is freed as conversion gain.
- **The [floor price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price) can rise under the curve assumptions.** Reducing supply while preserving reserve backing lifts the modeled floor. Conversions are one-way: POLAR cannot be converted back to pETH.

Image: The floor-price ratchet: burning pETH through conversions contracts supply along the power-law curve, lifting the floor from one level to a higher one; because conversions are one-way, the floor steps up and does not come back down

See [pETH Floor Price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price) for the full floor and floor-ratio explanation.

## The converter rebate

Before released ETH becomes system yield, a slice can return to the converter. The **converter rebate** is a stewardship-controlled dial between conversion volume and system yield:

Rebate | Effect

**0%** | Maximum yield to the system; conversions cost the most. Sensible when POLAR's market cap exceeds pETH's.
**100%** | Zero system yield; conversions are cheapest — the rebate returns the burned pETH's floor value, so the converter's net cost is only the premium above floor (at a 60% [floor ratio](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price), roughly 40% of the pETH's market value). An emergency lever to drive volume when the floor is stalling.
**In between** | The usual operating range, shifting with market conditions.

Stewards watch the **POLAR / pETH market-cap ratio**: when POLAR's is larger, a lower rebate is easier to defend; when it flips, higher rebates may support conversion volume.

> **Note:**
> The total ETH released from the curve is **unaffected** by the rebate in the model. The floor-price impact is the same however the ETH is split afterward, assuming the contracts and curve math execute as designed. The rebate reshapes the *distribution* of value, not the underlying collateral dynamics.

After the rebate, the remaining ETH is swapped into pETH and forwarded to the **[Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router)**, which buffers it and distributes the yield to pETH troves, pro-rata to their recorded debt.

## What conversion does *not* do

- **It does not manufacture demand for POLAR.** If POLAR trades below the conversion price, no conversions happen until decay catches up.
- **Parameters carry weight.** Decay too fast can cause frequent dilution; decay too slow can stall conversion activity. Final guardrails remain pending.

## How to convert

Conversions are a continuous, permissionless auction tuned for arbitrage bots and MEV searchers, so converting profitably means competing with them. Most users will hold POLAR, use current POLAR Staking where the app exposes it, or wait for [vePOLAR](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar) locking instead of converting directly.

**You need** pETH to burn (bought on the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) or a DEX) and a wallet connected to the Polaris app. On Public Testnet 1 this is Sepolia-only; production conversions are not actionable until [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) publishes the official app, conversion contract, final parameters, and audit status.

### Open the conversion panel

It shows the live **conversion price** and POLAR's **market price** side by side.

### Watch the decaying price

The conversion price decays continuously and spikes upward each time someone converts. A conversion is profitable when it drifts **below** POLAR's market price; that spread is your margin.

### Read the quote

Enter a pETH amount. The panel previews the **POLAR you will receive**, the **ETH rebate** at the current [rebate](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions#the-converter-rebate) setting, and the resulting **price spike**. Because the spike scales with the fraction of POLAR supply minted, larger amounts price progressively worse; favor smaller increments.

### Decide

Convert only while the previewed POLAR (plus rebate) beats acquiring POLAR on the open market. If the spread has closed, wait for decay to reopen it.

### Burn pETH ⇒ receive POLAR

Confirm the transaction. Your pETH is **permanently burned**, you receive newly minted POLAR, and any ETH rebate goes to your wallet.

### Stake, future-lock, or hold

Stake POLAR where the current app exposes POLAR Staking, wait for [vePOLAR](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar) locking once audited deployment publishes it, or hold it.

## pAsset Redemptions

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations.md

> **Actionability:** Redemptions are testnet-actionable only on Sepolia using test assets and current manifests. Production redemptions are not mainnet-actionable until [Polaris Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) publishes the official app, production addresses, final fee formula, and audit status.

## What a redemption is

A **redemption** exchanges a pAsset for the **[pETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth)** collateral backing it at the pAsset's protocol reference value, minus a fee. It pays when the pAsset trades *below* peg by more than the fee, gas, slippage, and execution risk.

If pUSD slips to \$0.98, a redeemer can buy pUSD cheaply, redeem it for about \$1 of pETH minus the fee, and sell or hold the pETH. The pETH comes from the collateral of troves carrying that pUSD debt, and their debt shrinks by the pUSD redeemed. No counterparty agrees to the trade; the protocol enforces it.

This puts an arbitrage floor under the pAsset's market price.

## Parameters

Redemption fees, decay factors, and current network values live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). This page explains the mechanism; the parameter page owns the numbers.

## How it restores the peg

A below-peg pAsset is a standing arbitrage. Walk the loop:

1. **Buy** pUSD on the market at \$0.98.
2. **Redeem** it for \$1 − fee of pETH.
3. **Sell** the pETH, pocket the spread.
4. Repeat until the arb closes.

Each pass buys pUSD on the open market, applying **upward price pressure**. Arbitrageurs keep going until pUSD is back at peg and the trade stops paying. Self-interest restores the peg, not intervention.

> **Note:** Redemptions handle the *below*-peg case. The *above*-peg case is handled by the inverse mechanism, **minting**, where arbitrageurs add pETH to troves to mint pAssets when the pAsset trades over peg. See [Minting pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting).

The two mechanisms are mirror images:

Mechanism | Direction | Arbitrageur does | Effect on troves

**Redemption** | pAsset < peg | Burns pAsset (repays debt) ⇒ takes pETH | Debt and collateral both shrink
**Minting** | pAsset > peg | Adds pETH ⇒ mints pAsset | Debt and collateral both grow

Their volumes also serve as the protocol's peg signal: a spike in redemptions reads as a downward depeg, a spike in mintings as an upward one. That is how Polaris sets [autonomous interest rates](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting) without a direct pUSD market-price feed.

Image: Interest rate and peg stability: above peg, minting volume rises and the interest rate falls to expand supply; below peg, redemption volume rises and the interest rate climbs to contract supply — each loop pushing the price back toward peg

*Redemption and minting volume are the onchain peg signal; the loops above show the mechanism's direction, not exact arithmetic.*

## Mechanics in detail

### Fees and fill limits

Current fee floors, decay factors, and fill constraints live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). The user-facing rule is simple: the redeemer receives the redeemed amount's reference value in pETH **minus** the fee and execution costs. If the fee, gas, pETH slippage, MEV risk, or price movement exceed the below-peg spread, the redemption is unprofitable.

Two contract-level details refine that rule. The fee is not paid out to anyone: the collateral it represents stays in the branch, accruing pro-rata to the remaining troves. And if the branch's [reserve-to-debt ratio (TCR)](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode) is below 100%, the post-fee proceeds are scaled down by that ratio — redeeming against a thinly backed branch returns proportionally less pETH.

The app should show a pre-confirmation quote once the interface is live. Treat examples here as arithmetic only.

### Borrower impact

A redemption targets the whole pAsset system, not a specific trove. It reduces debt and pETH collateral across **all** open troves for that pAsset, pro-rata to each trove's recorded collateral. There is no LTV queue, no "riskiest trove first," and no health metric that exempts a trove.

Being redeemed against is not a liquidation penalty: your debt is repaid at face value with your own collateral, so net equity is broadly unchanged before fees and rounding. The cost is control. Your trove becomes smaller, your pETH exposure falls, and future pETH-linked yield potential falls with it.

One edge case: because both reductions are shared pro-rata to collateral, a trove holding a lot of collateral relative to its debt can shed more debt than it owes. Debt floors at zero — the protocol mints the overshoot to the trove owner as pAsset the next time the trove is touched.

Lower LTV protects against **liquidation**, not redemption. It can still matter after a redemption, because the remaining LTV often improves but the position is smaller.

### MEV and transaction ordering

Redemptions are visible onchain transactions. Searchers can compete for the same spread, the pETH exit price can move, and system totals can shift before your transaction lands. Use the app's slippage limits, fee quotes, and any private-transaction support.

## Worked example: redemption impact

Assume pUSD trades at \$0.98, the redemption fee quote is 0.5%, and gas/slippage are ignored. A redeemer buys 10,000 pUSD for \$9,800 and redeems it for \$10,000 of pETH minus the 0.5% fee, or about \$9,950 of pETH. The gross spread is about \$150 before gas, slippage, MEV, and price movement.

Now the borrower side of the same redemption. The 10,000 pUSD redeemed reduces total system debt by 10,000 pUSD and removes roughly \$10,000 of pETH from the pool. Both reductions are shared across every open trove in proportion to recorded collateral.

For intuition, here is the proportional share for one trove holding a fifth of the system's collateral, so it absorbs a fifth of the redemption (2,000 pUSD of debt and about \$2,000 of pETH):

Step | Collateral value | Debt | LTV

Before redemption | \$18,000 | 12,000 pUSD | 66.7%
After absorbing its pro-rata share | About \$16,000 | 10,000 pUSD | 62.5%

The borrower did not repay voluntarily; the market did it through the redemption path. The trove is slightly safer by LTV, but smaller and less exposed to pETH.

## Before you act

1. Track your **LTV** with enough buffer for price moves, interest accrual, and redemption impact.
2. Treat a below-peg pAsset as a live signal that redemptions may shrink your position.

## Polaris Liquidations

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations.md

## What a liquidation is

A **liquidation** closes a trove whose **LTV exceeds the maximum** — the point at which its collateral no longer comfortably covers its debt. The protocol cancels the trove's debt and seizes its pETH collateral.

The purpose is solvency. Every pAsset is a claim on collateral; leaving undercollateralized troves open would leave the pAsset not fully backed. Liquidations are how Polaris is designed to keep backing intact, subject to functioning contracts, live Ethereum execution, available gas, valid pricing data, keeper/executor availability, and audited invariants.

Unlike a [redemption](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations), a liquidation is not an arbitrage you can opt into at par — it is a forced close triggered by your own LTV crossing a hard line.

## Where the debt and collateral go

Liquidation runs in two tiers.

1. **The Stability Pool absorbs it first.** The [Stability Pool](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield) holds a reserve of the pAsset. When a trove is liquidated, the pool burns pAsset equal to the trove's debt and receives pETH collateral covering the debt plus the liquidation penalty (any surplus goes back to the borrower — see below). Because liquidated troves are typically seized while still *overcollateralized* but past the LTV liquidation line, the pool usually receives more value in pETH than the pAsset it burns. That surplus is the depositors' compensation.

2. **Redistribution is the fallback.** If the Stability Pool is empty or too small to cover the debt, the trove's debt *and* collateral spread across the remaining troves on that pAsset, in proportion to their size. Other borrowers take on a slice of debt and a matching slice of pETH. The system stays solvent either way.

Path | Trigger | Absorbs debt | Receives collateral

**Stability Pool** | Pool has sufficient pAsset | Pool burns its pAsset | Depositors, pro-rata
**Redistribution** | Pool empty / insufficient | Other troves take it on | Other troves, pro-rata

**Collateral surplus.** When the Stability Pool absorbs a liquidation, the trove surrenders only what covers its debt, a liquidation penalty, and gas compensation for the liquidator. Any collateral beyond that goes to the **Collateral Surplus Pool**, where the former borrower can claim it. The claim is an explicit transaction — the surplus is not pushed back automatically, and it waits in the pool until claimed. (Collateral spread through the redistribution fallback is not returned this way.)

## Values to check

Current MCR, Recovery Mode, fee, and liquidation values live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). This page explains the closeout path; the parameter page owns the numbers.

Before acting, check:

- The pAsset's max LTV or inverse minimum collateral ratio.
- Any liquidation penalty, gas compensation, and keeper incentive.
- Stability Pool coverage and whether redistribution can apply.
- Oracle freshness and fallback behavior.

## Worked example: liquidation

The MCR number below is the current Public Testnet 1 pUSD/pGOLD value as of June 8, 2026. Parameters publish this as a 115% minimum collateral ratio, equivalent to about 87.0% max LTV. A borrower has 10,000 pUSD of debt and pETH collateral valued at \$11,300, so the LTV is about 88.5% — above the current testnet maximum, and liquidatable.

If the Stability Pool is large enough, it burns 10,000 pUSD and receives the trove's pETH. The borrower's debt is cleared; collateral covers the debt plus the contract-defined liquidation penalty and gas compensation where available, with any surplus returned for the borrower to claim. Depositors receive pETH pro rata; in a normal overcollateralized liquidation, that pETH should be worth more than the pUSD burned, but the exact gain depends on the penalty, execution timing, pETH price, gas, and slippage.

If the Stability Pool is empty or too small, the uncovered portion follows the redistribution path: remaining troves inherit a proportional slice of both the liquidated debt and collateral.

## Risks

Liquidation is involuntary and can happen fast. A liquidated trove gives up its collateral to cover the debt plus a liquidation penalty and gas compensation; any surplus is returned to you to claim, but the penalty makes liquidation a real loss to avoid. Keep a **buffer** below max LTV so a sharp move in collateral price does not cross the line. The thinner your margin, the less room you have when volatility hits. See Managing Your Trove.

You are the system's first-loss backstop. In a flash crash, troves can be liquidated near — or in extreme cases below — 100% collateralization, and you may receive pETH worth less than the pAsset burned. Over a full cycle the surplus from healthy liquidations compensates for that risk, but it is not riskless yield. See The Stability Pool and the Risk Disclosure.

## How liquidation interacts with system health

A trove's LTV is not all that matters. When system-wide backing runs thin, the protocol enters [Recovery Mode](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode), which tightens risk-increasing borrower operations while it lasts. Liquidation eligibility is unchanged: a trove is liquidated whenever its LTV exceeds the maximum, in every mode. Recovery Mode is the system-level safeguard complementing the trove-level liquidation described here.

## Polaris Recovery Mode

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode.md

## The Total Collateralization Ratio

The **Total Collateralization Ratio (TCR)** is the system-wide health metric for a pAsset. It is not a simple market-value-of-collateral over debt figure: it measures the bonding-curve reserve backing all the pETH collateral — the collateral scaled by the curve's reserve-backing fraction and valued at the reserve (redemption) price — divided by total pAsset debt outstanding.

$$
\text{TCR} = \frac{\text{Reserve backing the collateral} \times \text{reserve price}}{\text{Total debt}}
$$

A high TCR means a deep, well-backed system; a low TCR means the reserve behind that pAsset is thin relative to its debt. In the contracts this is the reserve-to-debt ratio, and crossing the threshold triggers the protective state (internally called Emergency Mode).

## What Recovery Mode is

**Recovery Mode** is a protective state entered automatically when TCR drops below a set threshold. It is not a freeze or shutdown; it tightens borrower actions that would make system backing thinner.

## Parameters

Recovery Mode thresholds and current per-pAsset values live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). This page explains behavior, not current numbers.

## How behavior changes

Recovery Mode does **not** change which troves are liquidatable. In every mode, a trove is liquidatable only once its LTV exceeds the normal maximum. What tightens is the set of borrower actions allowed while the state is active.

Your action | Normal Mode | Recovery Mode

**Add collateral / repay debt** | Allowed | Allowed
**Withdraw collateral / mint more debt** | Allowed if LTV stays below the maximum | Allowed only if the resulting LTV stays below the stricter recovery maximum and does not increase your LTV
**Liquidation eligibility** | LTV above the maximum | LTV above the maximum (unchanged)

The principle: actions that strengthen backing stay open; actions that thin backing face a higher bar. In practice, a collateral withdrawal or new mint on its own always raises LTV, so in Recovery Mode it goes through only as part of a combined adjustment that offsets it — adding collateral or repaying debt in the same transaction. Current trigger and action values are published on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

## Worked example: Recovery Mode

The trigger is the system's reserve-backing-to-debt ratio, so plain collateral value divided by debt is only a rough proxy. Treat the example as shape, not exact arithmetic.

Suppose the protective state is active for pUSD. A trove below the normal max LTV is **not** liquidated by Recovery Mode, but it may lose freedom to withdraw collateral or mint more debt. Those actions must clear the stricter recovery max LTV and cannot raise the trove's LTV. Adding collateral and repaying debt are always allowed.

The borrower's defense is the same as always: add collateral, repay debt, or both. For example, a trove with \$13,000 of collateral and 10,000 pUSD of debt is at 76.9% LTV; repaying 2,000 pUSD moves it to 8,000 / \$13,000, or 61.5% LTV, before fees and price movement — and lower LTV also widens your room to act while the system is stressed.

## How to make your trove safe

If the system is in or near Recovery Mode, the move is the same one that always protects a trove: lower your **LTV**.

1. **Add pETH collateral.** More collateral against the same debt lowers your LTV directly.
2. **Repay debt.** Burning pAsset to reduce what you owe lowers your LTV from the other side.
3. **Do both early.** Build the buffer before stress arrives, not while actions are already restricted.

See [Managing Your Trove](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove) for the mechanics, and [Liquidations](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations) for what happens to a trove that exceeds the max LTV.

> **Warning:** Recovery Mode is most likely during volatile conditions when adding collateral is hardest. The reliable defense is a buffer maintained in calm markets. See the [Risk Disclosure](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/risk-disclosure).

## Polaris Stewardship, not Governance

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship.md

Governance risk is structural: unlimited scope, undefined boundaries, and misaligned incentives create capture risk.

Polaris's answer is neither a fully immutable protocol with no adjustable parameters nor an unbounded DAO. It is a third path: an immutable core plus a clearly scoped layer where human judgment is necessary. We call it **stewardship** rather than governance because the difference is structural, not cosmetic.

> **Current status:** Stewardship is not live yet. POLAR and [vePOLAR](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar) contract addresses, proposal thresholds, voting periods, execution delays, and final numeric parameter bounds await audited deployment. These docs describe the intended scope and lifecycle without inventing launch parameters.

## The core is immutable

The mechanisms that define Polaris **cannot be changed by anyone**: not the team, not vePOLAR, not a vote. They are enforced by immutable contracts:

- The [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) backing pETH
- Branch logic and [trove](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove) accounting
- [Liquidations](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations)
- [Redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations)
- The [Stability Pool](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield)

The base protocol does not need to be relearned after each vote, because the core logic is out of reach by design.

## What stewardship controls

Stewardship operates at the edges: StablecoinOS onboarding, permitted parameter dials, the POLAR stewardship layer, fee and interest splits, [Flow](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router) participation, and treasury programs. Safety-critical adjustable values ship with hardcoded limits; the remaining numeric bounds are set at audited deployment.

Domain | Power | Constraint

**pAsset licensing** | Approve a team or branch to deploy a new pAsset on Polaris infrastructure | Standardized eligibility and launch requirements
**Branch parameters** | Adjust permitted fee and distribution splits for a pAsset branch | Hardcoded min/max bounds
**Conversion parameters** | Tune pETH-to-POLAR conversion settings (rebate, half-life, spike sensitivity) | Rebate bounded 0–100%; other bounds pending
**StablecoinOS onboarding** | Whitelist eligible participants into Flow revenue sharing | Must build on pETH/pAssets and meet published requirements
**Primary interest split** | Set how primary interest is split between the Stability Pool and POLAR stakers (remainder to the ecosystem fund) | Stability Pool and stakers each floored at 25% (hardcoded)
**Flow weights** | Set whitelisted recipient weights and related edge parameters | Hardcoded limits; no gauges, bribes, or emissions
**Treasury programs** | Operate programs such as the Polar Council | Initially team-managed until a framework is published

A stewardship vote can bring a new pAsset or Flow participant into the system, or move an approved parameter within its range. It cannot alter the bonding curve, redemption mechanism, liquidation logic, Stability Pool mechanics, or core contract immutability.

## What stewardship does not do

Polaris stewardship does **not**:

- Upgrade or replace immutable core contracts.
- Add admin keys, pause powers, freeze powers, or blacklists.
- Create POLAR emissions gauges.
- Route bribes or vote-buying markets through the protocol.
- Mint POLAR outside the pETH conversion mechanism.
- Set safety-critical parameters (such as interest-rate maxima, interest-split floors, and fee or rebate ceilings) outside their hardcoded bounds.
- Change a user's trove, Stability Pool deposit, pETH balance, or lock by discretion.

## Operator prerequisites

Teams seeking onboarding, a pAsset branch, Flow participation, or another stewarded action should expect to provide:

- Clear contract addresses once deployed and verified.
- A concise description of the product, risk surface, and user flow.
- The exact stewarded action requested.
- The parameter values requested, with the published min/max bounds shown beside them.
- Evidence that the action cannot touch immutable core logic.
- Security status: audits, tests, admin roles, upgradeability, and emergency controls for the requesting team's own contracts.
- Revenue-sharing, Flow, or integration assumptions, if relevant.
- Monitoring and reporting commitments after approval.

The final submission template is pending. Until it is published, operators should prepare around these fields rather than assume an informal path.

## Proposal and voting lifecycle

The final stewardship UI and contract-level process are pending. The intended lifecycle is:

Stage | Purpose

**Draft** | Describe the request, affected contracts, user impact, risks, and requested parameters
**Bounds check** | Confirm each requested value sits inside immutable min/max limits
**Security review** | Confirm verified source, audit status, admin roles, and integration risks
**Vote** | vePOLAR holders vote according to the published lock and vote-power rules
**Queue / execution** | Approved changes are queued and executed through the published contract path
**Verification** | Docs, indexers, and monitors confirm the new state onchain
**Monitoring** | Operators and stewards watch the result and propose reversals or adjustments if needed

No proposal is executable until the official process, contracts, and parameter bounds are published.

## The three-tier decision model

Polaris follows a simple ordering: solve a problem at the lowest tier that can solve it, and escalate only what the tier below cannot handle.

1. **Automate what you can.** Interest-rate setting, floor-price growth, liquidation processing, and redemptions run autonomously.
2. **Incentivize what you can't automate.** Where behavior can't be hardcoded but can be paid for, durable protocol revenue and Flow formulas handle it.
3. **Steward only what's left.** Only decisions that require judgment go to vePOLAR.

## Bounded blast radius

Stewards hold real power, so they can make real mistakes, but the immutable core caps the damage. A weak pAsset affects that branch, not the bonding curve or liquidation engine. A poor split can be moved back inside the same hardcoded bounds.

## How this compares to DAO governance

Dimension | Unbounded DAO governance | Polaris stewardship

**Scope** | Open-ended; can reach the base protocol | Enumerated and bounded
**Core** | Mutable via vote | Immutable, out of reach of any vote
**Incentives** | Often routed through emissions, gauges, and bribes | Routed through protocol revenue, configurable revenue splits, and Flows
**Failure mode** | Base-protocol denaturation, capture, extraction | Bounded mistakes at the edges

The lesson Polaris draws: bound the scope first. Less can be captured, overpaid for, or changed away from the base design.

## Structure over trust

Stewardship is not a promise to behave well; it is a structure with a bounded surface. The core is immutable. Each lever is bounded, auditable, and reversible where the underlying contract permits. Team influence is diluted through the [conversion mechanism](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions) as new POLAR enters circulation.

## Fee Router & Flows

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router.md

This page covers two related systems:

- **Fee Router:** internal revenue plumbing for protocol yield.
- **Flows:** formula-based revenue sharing for approved integrations.

> **Current status:** Fee Router addresses, ABIs, events, distribution cadence, Flow whitelists, and final parameter bounds are pending audited deployment. Use [Contracts & Addresses](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#contracts-and-addresses) and [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters) for published values.

## Fee Router

The Fee Router receives protocol revenue, buffers it, and sends it to the configured recipient path. It moves existing assets only; it does not mint POLAR or create emissions.

### Inputs

Source | What enters

[Conversion auctions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions) | pETH purchased with the released ETH, after any [converter rebate](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions#the-converter-rebate)
Bonding-curve swap fees | pETH fees from ETH ⇄ pETH activity

These are the only two sources wired into the router today; borrower interest follows a separate pAsset-denominated path (see [Yield Sources](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources)).

Swap fees take one extra step before distribution: accumulated fee pETH is **burned at the floor price**, which lifts the [floor](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price) and releases reserve ETH; that ETH is swapped back into pETH and distributed with the rest. The burn is the floor-raising mechanism — what reaches the destinations below is the repurchased pETH, not the burned fees. Anyone can trigger the burn-and-repurchase step; sending buffered pETH onward to a destination is a stewardship action.

For the full source matrix, see [Yield Sources](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources).

### Outputs

Destination | What they receive

Troves | pETH yield credited pro-rata to recorded debt
Flow recipients | Revenue-sharing distributions for whitelisted contracts (forthcoming)

POLAR stakers are not paid by the Fee Router: their share comes from **borrower interest** through the separate interest router. See [POLAR Tokenomics](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/tokenomics#where-the-yield-comes-from).

The Fee Router buffers incoming value before distribution. Buffering smooths bursty conversions and swap fees into steadier accounting periods.

The **converter rebate** sits upstream of the router. It controls how much released ETH returns to the converter before the rest becomes system yield. See [Conversion Auctions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions#the-converter-rebate).

## Operator checks

Before relying on the Fee Router, operators and indexers should verify:

- Router address, source, and ABI.
- Published input sources.
- Current recipient path and any min/max bounds.
- Distribution cadence and claim path.
- Events for deposits, buffering, distributions, claims, and parameter changes.
- Audit notes specific to router accounting.

Indexers should track inputs by source, buffered amounts before distribution, each distribution or claim, and the parameter values active during the distribution.

## Flows

Flows share protocol revenue with approved contracts that create durable demand for Polaris assets. They are designed to replace recurring token emissions with revenue paid in pETH or pAssets.

The base rule:

```text
Flow share = contract's weighted asset held / total weighted asset held across whitelisted contracts
```

If a whitelisted contract holds 20% of the whitelisted pETH supply, it receives 20% of that Flow before launch-specific adjustments.

## Flow families

Flow | Recipients | Funded by

pETH Flow | Eligible pETH-holding contracts | Bonding-curve and conversion-related revenue
pAsset Flow | Eligible pAsset-holding contracts | Borrower interest or other pAsset revenue after required Stability Pool allocation

Each Flow has its own whitelist, observed asset, recipient rules, and parameter bounds. Final contracts will define which assets, recipients, and revenue sources go live.

## Stewarded edges

The Flow formula should run automatically once configured. Stewardship is limited to edge decisions:

- Admit or remove a contract from a whitelist.
- Set a bounded weight or kickstart where allowed.
- Distinguish observed address from recipient address where contracts support it.
- Adjust entries inside published bounds.

Flow eligibility is not automatic. Operators should prepare product details, verified contracts, admin/upgrade model, requested Flow, monitoring plan, and user risk disclosure.

## Flow lifecycle

Stage | What happens

Application | Operator submits product, contracts, requested Flow, and risk details
Review | Stewards check eligibility, security posture, and bounds
Vote | vePOLAR votes on whitelist admission and settings
Activation | Recipient is added through the official execution path
Distribution | Formula allocates revenue by holdings, weights, and active parameters
Monitoring | Holdings, distributions, and risks are tracked

## Flows vs emissions

Aspect | Emission gauges | Polaris Flows

Currency | Governance token emissions | pETH or pAssets
Funding | Inflation | Protocol revenue
Allocation | Recurring votes and bribes | Formula based on eligible holdings
Stewardship load | Ongoing weight wars | Onboarding plus bounded adjustments

The only intended path for new POLAR after inception is [pETH conversion](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions). Flows use protocol revenue instead.

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar.md

**vePOLAR is vote-escrowed POLAR.** Lock [POLAR](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar) to receive stewardship rights for bounded parameters and edge decisions.

> **Current status:** vePOLAR is not the same as current **POLAR Staking**. The app may expose stake/unstake/claim actions for POLAR rewards on testnet, while the vePOLAR locking interface, contract address, proposal thresholds, and final yield split are pending. The lock design itself (durations, decay, delegation) is committed — see [Lock status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar#lock-status). Do not lock POLAR through an unofficial interface.

## What vePOLAR does

```
lock POLAR -> receive vePOLAR -> steward bounded parameters + receive routed yield
```

vePOLAR holders are **stewards, not governors**. They steer quantitative parameters within hardcoded ranges and vote on permitted onboarding decisions — pAsset licensing, StablecoinOS and [Flow](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router) onboarding, bounded splits and branch parameters, [conversion](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions) tuning, and treasury programs once the framework is published. The full enumeration of powers and constraints lives in [Stewardship, not Governance](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship); the bonding curve, branch logic, liquidations, redemptions, and Stability Pool mechanics are outside vote scope.

## POLAR Staking is separate

POLAR Staking is a staking/rewards surface: stake POLAR, unstake where allowed, and claim routed rewards. It does not by itself create vote-escrowed vePOLAR, lock-duration voting power, or stewardship rights.

vePOLAR is the future locking layer. The committed lock design is in [Lock status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar#lock-status) below; once live, this page will add the official lock contract, proposal thresholds, and yield claim path.

## What vePOLAR does not do

vePOLAR cannot touch the immutable core: no contract upgrades, no gauges or bribe markets, no POLAR minting outside the [conversion auction](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions), no parameter moved outside its immutable min/max range. The full no-list is on [Stewardship, not Governance](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship).

## Why Polaris Finance

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/why-polaris
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/why-polaris.md

DeFi should not end as a thin interface over the same institutions it was built to route around. Yet the market has drifted in that direction: more tokenized T-bills, more custodial yield, more proxy contracts, more multisigs, and more black-box counterparties sitting between users and the assets they think they own.

Polaris exists to move yield back onchain.

## The wrong direction

DeFi has become increasingly centralized and tethered to traditional finance for yield. The two dominant sources are:

1. **RWAs and T-bills for stablecoins.** Tokenized offchain assets import banking, custody, issuer, blacklist, jurisdiction, and redemption risk.
2. **ETH staking for ETH assets.** Liquid staking gives useful base yield, but it has also centralized around a small set of staking providers, node operators, and restaking-style dependencies.

These are useful products, but they should not be accepted as the final state of DeFi. They make yield depend on institutions, legal structures, privileged operators, or offchain promises. That dependency may look efficient in calm markets, but it reintroduces the same risks DeFi was supposed to make optional: frozen assets, opaque balance sheets, socialized losses, discretionary governance, and changing rules after users have committed capital.

The extra yield does not compensate users for all of those risks. It often hides them.

## What DeFi still needs

The internet needs a financial base layer that is hard to capture.

It needs money and yield that can move between humans, companies, agents, protocols, and nation states without depending on a bank account, custodian, issuer, or committee that can be pressured into changing the rules. It needs infrastructure that can serve users across borders without assuming that every user, jurisdiction, and counterparty will remain politically convenient forever.

That base layer cannot be built from assets that can be frozen, confiscated, blacklisted, upgraded into a different risk profile, or made dependent on offchain redemption. It has to be:

- **Immutable at the core,** so users can rely on the rules they entered under.
- **Counterparty-free,** so yield does not depend on a custodian, issuer, exchange, bank, or market maker.
- **Onchain by default,** so value accrues from transparent protocol activity rather than hidden balance sheets.
- **Productive,** so users do not have to choose between sovereignty and useful yield.

Polaris is built for that version of DeFi.

## The Polaris answer

Polaris turns onchain financial activity into a yield layer.

Its core asset, [pETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth), is created through a bonding curve. Activity on that curve builds reserves, raises the floor, and creates yield-bearing collateral that can support new onchain assets. From there, Polaris extends into [pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/passet-catalog): synthetic assets backed by pETH, with borrower interest, Stability Pool flows, conversion gains, and curve activity feeding the system instead of an offchain desk.

The point is not simply to create another stablecoin. The point is to create a base layer where collateral, synthetic money, and yield are generated by immutable onchain mechanisms rather than imported from centralized intermediaries.

## Design choices

Polaris makes a few deliberate tradeoffs to keep the core useful without reintroducing offchain dependency.

Choice | Why it exists | What it enables

**One collateral primitive: pETH** | A single reserve asset keeps risk accounting legible instead of fragmenting collateral across many tokens. | Every pAsset branch shares the same collateral base and yield engine.
**Two-way bonding curve** | The curve gives pETH contract-native liquidity without depending on external liquidity providers. | ETH can enter and exit through transparent onchain rules while fees and burns can raise the pETH floor.
**pAssets as branch debt** | Each pAsset gets its own branch, parameters, Stability Pool, and redemption path instead of sharing one pooled debt bucket. | Polaris can support multiple synthetic assets while isolating branch-level risk.
**Minting and redemptions as peg signals** | Peg pressure is visible in protocol actions, so rates can react to onchain demand instead of discretionary committees. | pAssets get autonomous incentives around both sides of the peg.
**POLAR outside the immutable core** | Growth and stewardship should not be required for the base protocol to function. | POLAR can coordinate outward-facing incentives while pETH, pAssets, liquidations, and redemptions keep operating independently.
**Bounded stewardship** | Parameters need tuning, but users need confidence that the core cannot be rewritten underneath them. | Stewardship can adjust quantitative settings inside predefined limits without becoming unbounded governance.

## Why it matters

If DeFi's main yield sources remain T-bills and staking wrappers, then DeFi becomes a distribution layer for other people's balance sheets. That is not enough for a global financial system, and it is especially not enough for a future where autonomous agents, cross-border communities, and ordinary users need assets that cannot be politically switched off.

Polaris is a bet that the next yield layer should be native to crypto's strongest properties:

- transparent execution,
- immutable rules,
- self-custody,
- permissionless access,
- and economic activity that compounds inside the system itself.

That is the vision: not DeFi as a wrapper around tradfi yield, but DeFi as its own yield-bearing monetary foundation.

## Polaris Stability Pool

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield.md

The Stability Pool is a **first-loss pool**. When a trove is liquidated, the pool burns pAssets to cover the debt and receives pETH collateral in return. Your deposit can be partially consumed and replaced by pETH. This is the mechanism, not an edge case.

## What the Stability Pool does

The Stability Pool keeps Polaris solvent. It holds pAsset deposits that are used to absorb liquidated trove debt. In exchange, depositors receive the pETH collateral seized from liquidated troves, usually at a discount to market price. This creates an alignment of incentives: depositors profit from liquidations, which keeps the system healthy.

Image: How the Stability Pool fits the system: ETH enters the bonding curve as pETH, which collateralizes the pUSD branch and its user CDPs; pUSD deposited in the Stability Pool absorbs liquidated debt and earns discounted pETH plus a share of borrower interest

## Where the yield comes from

Depositors earn from two streams:

1. **Borrower interest.** A share of the pAsset-denominated interest paid by all troves borrowing that pAsset routes to the pool. This is the steady-state base yield.
2. **Liquidation gains.** When a trove is liquidated, the pool absorbs its debt (burning depositors' pAssets) and receives its pETH collateral. The collateral value at the moment of liquidation typically exceeds the absorbed debt, so the net position improves — but the composition shifts from pAssets to pETH.

The interest share is the steady base; liquidation gains spike when markets are turbulent. Together they make the Stability Pool the protocol's primary yield venue for pAssets. Current split values live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

## First-loss mechanics

When a liquidation occurs:

- The pool burns enough pAssets to match the trove's debt.
- The pool receives the trove's pETH collateral.
- Every depositor's pAsset balance shrinks proportionally; every depositor's pETH balance grows proportionally.

**Before depositing:** your share can be drawn down at any time when any trove carrying that pAsset's debt crosses the liquidation line. The pool does not protect individuals from this; it distributes it equally. The risk is priced into the liquidation discount — on average, the pETH you receive is worth more than the pAssets you gave up — but realized gain depends on ETH price movement between the liquidation and when you exit.

## Wrapper vaults

Raw Stability Pool mechanics leave depositors holding pETH after liquidations. Wrapper vaults are an anticipated abstraction above the raw pool, but they are not a live product and details are not published.

## Risk

The Stability Pool is the protocol's *intended* first-loss layer. Key risks:

- **pETH price exposure.** Liquidation gains arrive as pETH, which is volatile against ETH. A large liquidation event that converts your full deposit to pETH, followed by a pETH price drop, can leave you worse off in ETH terms.
- **Extreme drawdown.** If a large fraction of all troves are liquidated simultaneously (e.g., a flash crash before the liquidation engine processes them), the pool may not fully cover all debt. In that case the protocol falls back to redistribution — redistributing remaining collateral and debt across surviving troves — and the pool is partially or fully depleted. A crashing trove can even fall below 100% collateralization before processing; the pool then absorbs more debt than the collateral it receives is worth, and that underwater loss is shared proportionally by all depositors.
- **Smart-contract risk.** The pool is a smart contract; a bug can affect any depositor. Mitigated by audits, never zero.

## How to deposit

You need a pAsset to deposit, usually **pUSD** ([mint some](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/open-a-trove) if you hold none), plus ETH for gas. Re-read the pre-deposit warning before signing.

### Open the pool

Open the Polaris app and go to the **Stability Pool** for your pAsset (e.g. the pUSD pool).

### Enter a deposit amount

Enter the amount of pUSD to deposit.

### Approve the spend

**Approve** the pUSD spend, if prompted (a one-time token approval).

### Confirm the deposit

Confirm the **deposit** transaction in your wallet.

## Polaris Yield Sources

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources.md

Polaris yield comes from onchain protocol activity: borrowing, liquidations, swaps, and conversions. This page lists the sources; [Fee Router & Flows](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router) covers routing.

> Adoption ⇒ activity ⇒ velocity ⇒ fees ⇒ yield ⇒ more demand.

## The sources

Source | Where it originates | Flows to

**Borrower interest** | Interest [trove](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove) owners pay on minted pAssets | Split by the interest router between [Stability Pool](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield) depositors (currently the largest share), POLAR stakers, and an ecosystem remainder — stewarded above hardcoded floors
**Liquidation gains** | The penalty on liquidated troves, paid in discounted pETH | Stability Pool depositors
**Bonding curve swap fees** | Fees on every ETH ⇄ pETH swap, buffered by the Fee Router and later burned at the [floor price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price) | The pETH floor (via the burn); the released ETH is recycled into pETH and distributed to troves
**Conversion gains** | ETH released when pETH is burned to mint POLAR in a [conversion auction](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions) | The Fee Router, which recycles it into pETH and streams it to troves pro-rata to recorded debt

The first two drive [Stability Pool](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield) yield and are the primary venue for pAsset holders. The bonding curve and conversion components make **pETH used as trove collateral yield-bearing** — the yield accrues to troves and to pETH's rising floor, not to a passive token balance — which is why using pETH as collateral can lower a borrower's net effective cost.

## Why this is "self-scaling"

These sources depend on protocol **activity**:

- More troves opened ⇒ more borrower interest.
- More volatility ⇒ more liquidation gains.
- More trading ⇒ more bonding curve swap fees.
- More POLAR minted ⇒ more conversion gains.

Yield scales with pAsset supply and protocol use, but realized returns still depend on activity, rates, liquidations, contract execution, and market conditions.

## Owned, not rented

Polaris yield is not subsidized, bridged in, or voted into being. It emerges from protocol use and is captured by the protocol itself.

## How yield is distributed

Conversion proceeds and swap fees go through the **[Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router)**. Borrower interest follows separate pAsset-denominated split logic. Current values and pending production status live on [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

## Parameters

Use [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters) for current yield-related values. This page owns the source model, not numeric tables.
