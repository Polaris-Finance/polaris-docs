# Understand Polaris

## Issuing pAssets

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting.md

Polaris lets users issue pAssets directly from the protocol.
To issue USDp or GOLDp, users deposit pETH as collateral and create an overcollateralized position. The issued asset can then be used across Polaris and the wider DeFi ecosystem.
This is similar in spirit to borrowing from a money market: you provide collateral, receive an asset, and manage your position over time. The difference is that pAssets are issued by the protocol against pETH collateral, rather than borrowed from a pool of external lenders.

## What To Consider Before Issuing
Before issuing pAssets, check:
- Your LTV and liquidation price.
- How pETH price exposure affects your collateral.
- How pETH incentives may accrue based on your issued debt.
- How minting and redemptions can affect your position when pAssets trade above or below peg.
- Whether the system is in a normal or recovery state.

Issuing pAssets creates an active position. You should monitor it over time.

## Basic Flow
1. Acquire pETH through Swap or another supported route.
2. Open Issue.
3. Choose USDp or GOLDp.
4. Enter pETH collateral and the amount to issue.
5. Review LTV, liquidation price, expected cost, and incentives.
6. Approve pETH if needed, then sign.

For current LTV thresholds and fees, see [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). For the full risk model, see [Risk Disclosure](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/risk-disclosure).

## Interest Rates

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/interest-rates
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/interest-rates.md

Every open position pays interest on its pAsset debt. Polaris does not set this rate by hand or by vote. Two autonomous rates run side by side: a **primary rate** that leans against peg deviation, and a **secondary rate** that responds to how heavily the system is borrowed. Together they are the protocol's main tool for defending each pAsset's peg without offchain promises.

Your total rate is simply the two added together, accruing continuously on your debt.

## The two rates at a glance

Rate property | Primary rate | Secondary rate

Driven by | Net issuance vs. redemption flow (the peg signal) | The system's debt-to-reserve ratio
Updates | On every mint or redemption | Permissionlessly, and on every debt change
Who pays | Every open position, on its debt | Every open position, on its debt
Where it goes | Routed out as protocol revenue | Redistributed inside the system, by collateral
Hard cap | 250% APR | 250% APR

## Primary rate - flow-driven

The protocol treats net issuance and redemption as its onchain peg signal, and the primary rate leans against it:

- **Net redemptions push the rate up.** Redemptions tend to dominate when a pAsset trades below peg. A higher borrow cost discourages new issuance and rewards repayment, contracting supply back toward the peg.
- **Net minting pulls the rate down.** Issuance tends to dominate when a pAsset trades above peg. A lower borrow cost invites more supply, easing the price back down.

Mechanically, each mint or redeem nudges the rate by the log of the resulting supply change, scaled by a steward-set sensitivity. The rate is an evolving level, not a fresh calculation each block, and it is clamped to a configured minimum and maximum.

**Where primary interest goes.** Primary interest is real protocol revenue, paid in the pAsset on its own path - separate from the [Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router), which handles pETH swap and conversion revenue. As it accrues it is distributed three ways:

- **[Earn Vaults](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield)** - the first-loss backstop for liquidations. At least 25%.
- **[vePOLAR](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/vepolar)** - lockers' guaranteed minimum share of issuer interest. At least 25%.
- **[pAsset Flows](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router#flows)** - the remainder funds revenue-sharing for approved contracts building on Polaris.

Stewards can adjust the split, but both 25% floors are enforced in code.

## Secondary rate - collateralization-driven

The secondary rate responds not to flow but to the **debt-to-reserve ratio**: how much pAsset debt the system carries relative to the pETH reserve backing it. It follows a kinked curve - rising gently while the system is comfortably collateralized, then steeply once debt passes a configured kink point. Like the primary rate it is capped at 250% APR.

It can be refreshed by anyone, permissionlessly (for example after a price move), and it also refreshes on every debt change, so it tracks conditions without waiting on a keeper.

**Where secondary interest goes.** Unlike the primary rate, the secondary rate is not extracted as revenue - it stays inside the system. It is charged on every position's debt and credited back to positions in proportion to their **collateral**. In aggregate it nets to zero. The net effect is a redistribution from high-LTV positions to conservatively collateralized ones, and that pull strengthens as the whole system becomes more heavily borrowed. It is, in effect, an internal cost for running hot when the protocol can least afford it.

> **Note:** Because secondary interest is redistributive, a well-collateralized position can come out ahead on it while a position near its maximum LTV pays in.

## What a borrower experiences

Both rates accrue on your debt at once, annualized and continuous. The practical effects:

- Your debt grows over time, so your [LTV](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove) drifts upward even if you take no action.
- Adding collateral or repaying debt lowers your exposure to both rates; the secondary rate in particular eases as your LTV drops.
- The combined rate is visible onchain as the sum of the primary and secondary rates.

## Bounds and stewardship

Both rates are hard-capped at **250% APR** in the contracts, and that ceiling cannot be raised by stewards or by vote. Within those bounds stewards tune only quantitative parameters: the primary rate's sensitivity, the secondary rate's curve (minimum, maximum, kink, and slopes), and the primary revenue split (subject to the 25% floors). The rate logic itself cannot be rewritten. See [Audits & Security](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/audits-security) for the full scope of bounded parameters.

## Launch a pAsset

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/launch-a-passet
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/launch-a-passet.md

Polaris is **debt-asset agnostic**: it can issue a synthetic pegged to any asset with robust reference pricing and risk parameters. External teams can launch new pAsset markets on the same pETH collateral base, without bootstrapping a fresh collateral pool.

> **Current status:** USDp and GOLDp are the Testnet-active pAssets. New pAsset onboarding runs through stewardship, whose process and parameter bounds are pending audited deployment. See [Stewardship](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship).

## StablecoinOS: an open catalog

StablecoinOS lets new pAssets share the same pETH collateral pool and bonding curve. Shared collateral lets teams add a new pAsset without bootstrapping a fresh collateral base. Each pAsset still has its own debt accounting, so stress in one pAsset does not directly liquidate another pAsset's borrow positions.

Image: StablecoinOS as a factory: ETH enters one shared bonding curve to create pETH, which collateralizes multiple independent pAsset markets - USDp, GOLDp, pCHF - over the same pETH collateral base

## Opt into the yield layer and Flows

A pAsset built on Polaris inherits the protocol's yield plumbing: borrower interest and liquidation gains flow to its Earn Vault, and approved contracts building on the asset can earn a share of protocol revenue through [Flows](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router#flows) - revenue paid in pETH or pAssets instead of token emissions.

## What a pAsset market is, technically

Polaris is not one monolithic system. The pETH bonding curve and its collateral reserve are shared infrastructure, deployed once. Everything specific to a pAsset is a separate market stack, deployed per asset and wired to that shared curve. Launching pCHF, say, means deploying a new stack, not modifying USDp's.

A market stack is self-contained, with its own:

- Price feed for the tracked asset, composed with the pETH collateral price.
- pAsset token (an ERC-20), mintable only by its own market contracts.
- Earn Vault, the market's first-loss backstop and yield venue.
- Position manager and position NFT, tracking debt and collateral.
- Interest controller, holding the market's primary and secondary rates.
- Peg stability module, setting mint and redemption fees.
- Primary-interest router, splitting interest across Earn Vault, vePOLAR, and Flows.

Because each market keeps its own debt accounting, stress in one pAsset cannot directly liquidate positions in another. Markets share only the pETH collateral.

## What you configure

Most of the launch work is choosing parameters, all of which sit inside protocol-enforced bounds:

- Oracle: a robust, manipulation-resistant reference price for the tracked asset, read through a Medianiser. Because collateral is pETH priced in USD, every market also depends on the shared ETH/USD feed. This is the largest technical requirement.
- Collateral and liquidation: the minimum collateral ratio (maximum LTV), the emergency-mode thresholds, and the liquidation penalty.
- Interest rates: the primary rate band and its sensitivity, and the secondary-rate curve (minimum, maximum, kink, and slopes). Both rates are hard-capped at 250% APR. See [Interest Rates](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/interest-rates).
- Fees: the mint and redemption fee floors and their decay parameters.
- Revenue split: how the market's primary interest divides across the Earn Vault, vePOLAR, and Flows, subject to the 25% floors.

Stewardship reviews and approves these within hardcoded ranges. It cannot grant values outside them.

## Requirements

Polaris can support a new pAsset where the reference price and risk parameters are robust. A team launching a market should expect to demonstrate:

- **Oracle quality** - a reliable, manipulation-resistant reference price for the tracked asset.
- **Market demand** - evidence that the pAsset has real users and use cases.
- **Liquidity path** - a credible route to onchain liquidity for the pAsset.
- **Risk parameters** - collateral, fee, and liquidation settings that fit within the protocol's bounds.
- **UI / integration** - a usable interface and the integration work to expose the market.
- **Governance / stewardship** - a clean security posture and a path through the stewardship onboarding process.

These map to the stewardship onboarding criteria. For the full operator prerequisites, the pAsset-licensing scope, and the proposal lifecycle, see [Stewardship](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship#operator-prerequisites).

## The path to launch

Launching a pAsset is license-gated, not permissionless. The flow is a petition to stewardship:

1. Petition stewardship for a license, presenting a plan for the desired pAsset: the asset to track, its oracle, evidence of demand and a liquidity path, and the proposed parameters (see Requirements above).
2. Stewardship reviews the plan and grants or denies the license, weighing oracle quality, security posture, and whether the parameters sit within bounds.
3. Once licensed, the market stack is deployed against the shared bonding curve, stewardship, fee router, and share staking, and authorized through the official execution path.
4. Go live: issuers open positions against shared pETH, depositors use the new Earn Vault, and the market's interest plugs into the yield layer.

## Manage a Position

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove.md

[After you open a position](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/open-a-trove), you can adjust it: add collateral, issue more, repay debt, withdraw collateral, or close. The main app-facing health metric is loan-to-value (LTV).

## LTV

LTV is calculated by dividing the value of your debt by the value of your collateral:

LTV = debt face value ÷ collateral value

Lower LTV is safer. Some protocol references use collateral ratio instead, which is the reciprocal:

ICR = collateral value ÷ debt face value

That means a 150% collateral ratio is the same position as 66.7% LTV. Keep these in mind:

- Above the maximum LTV, the position can be liquidated.
- New issuance and redemptions can change the balance between collateral and debt across open positions when a pAsset trades away from its peg.
- Your LTV can move even if you do nothing, because prices, rates, new issuance, redemptions, and rewards keep changing.
## Basic Actions
- Add collateral: lowers LTV without changing debt.
- Issue more: raises LTV and reduces your safety buffer.
- Repay debt: lowers LTV and frees room to withdraw collateral.
- Close: repay the full debt, then withdraw collateral.
- Transfer: positions are represented by an NFT. Transferring it transfers control of collateral, debt, and management rights.
## What To Watch
- Borrow interest increases debt over time.
- Your position can receive pETH incentives based on issued debt.
- New issuance can add collateral and debt to existing positions, potentially lowering LTV.
- Redemptions can reduce collateral and debt even while the position is solvent.
- Withdrawing collateral or issuing more debt raises LTV.
- Recovery Mode can restrict risk-increasing actions.
For current thresholds, see [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). For the full risk model, see [Risk Disclosure](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/risk-disclosure).

## GOLDp

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/pgold
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/pgold.md

GOLDp is a **pAsset that tracks the price of one ounce of gold**, issued against pETH collateral rather than bullion in a vault. It is decentralized gold exposure with no KYC, no size minimums, and no counterparty other than the Polaris protocol itself - no vault in Singapore, just a price feed and pETH collateral.

> **Note:** GOLDp is Testnet-active on Sepolia. See [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) for the current list of live pAssets.

Under current issuer terms, the two centralized tokens together hold roughly \$5B in market cap, and redemption is gated behind KYC plus fees and minimums - XAUT, for example, charges 0.25% and requires roughly one gold bar (430 XAUT, about \$2.1M) to redeem. ## Borrowing GOLDp is not shorting gold

Image: Borrowing GOLDp is not shorting gold: deposit pETH and borrow GOLDp, then your strategy sets the exposure - hold or LP the gold pair for gold-neutral exposure, or sell GOLDp for pETH (or loop) to take short-gold exposure

Issuing GOLDp against pETH is a borrowing action; whether you end up long pETH, gold-neutral, or short gold depends on what you do with the GOLDp. Borrow 1 GOLDp and hold it and your gold exposure is neutral - you owe 1 GOLDp and hold 1 GOLDp. The position only becomes a short if you sell that GOLDp for a non-gold asset.

So almost any view on gold can be expressed with GOLDp:

Your view | How to express it

**Long gold** | Buy GOLDp and LP a gold-correlated pair
**Short gold, long ETH** | Post pETH, borrow GOLDp, then sell it or loop the position
**Long gold, short ETH** | Buy GOLDp and deposit it in the [Earn Vault](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield)
**Gold-neutral, long volume** | Borrow GOLDp and LP gold-correlated pairs

## Why borrow GOLDp

Polaris is designed to make borrowing attractive: borrowers pay interest but also receive pETH incentives on their position, so the *effective* rate can run low or even negative when incentives exceed interest. Because gold sees strong holding demand but comparatively little borrow demand, GOLDp is a natural fit for that flexibility. See [Yield Sources](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources) and [POLAR Tokenomics](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/tokenomics) for the mechanics.

## USDp

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/pusd
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/pusd.md

USDp is Polaris's flagship **pAsset pegged to the US dollar**, issued against pETH collateral. It belongs to a rare class of stablecoins that carry **no counterparty dependency** - the same trust-minimized family as LUSD and BOLD. Its peg is not propped up by an offchain reserve that can be frozen or fail; it is held by onchain incentives.

> **Note:** USDp is Testnet-active on Sepolia. See [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) for the current list of live pAssets.

## Why use USDp

USDp's yield is **owned, not rented**. Where most stablecoin yield is borrowed from offchain sources (T-bills, CEX lending, custodial strategies) that can compress or disappear, USDp earns from the protocol's own activity - borrower interest and liquidation gains - so it scales with adoption rather than external conditions. See [Yield Sources](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources#owned-not-rented) for why this is self-scaling.

## How USDp is created

You issue USDp by depositing pETH collateral and opening a position - the same flow as any pAsset. Arbitrageurs can also issue or redeem USDp directly against pETH at the oracle price through the Peg Stability Module.

- Issue from a position: [Issuing pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting) → [Open a Position](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/open-a-trove).
- Direct PSM issue/redeem: [Advanced](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/using-app/advanced).

## How the peg is supported

Two opposing, permissionless arbitrage mechanisms defend the peg without anyone's approval:

- **Below peg**, [redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations) let anyone swap USDp for its pETH collateral at face value, buying USDp off the market until it returns to peg.
- **Above peg**, issuing new USDp against pETH expands supply back toward peg.

## pETH Bonding Curve

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth.md

pETH is the native collateral asset of Polaris.
The protocol needed collateral that could support scalable 1:1 pAsset issuance without introducing centralized counterparties, external liquidity dependence, or offchain yield.

Other ETH-denominated assets can add yield, but yield alone is not enough. Many still depend on staking infrastructure, external liquidity, upgradeable systems, or yield sources outside the Polaris system. They introduce new and varying risks without solving the core collateral problem.

pETH was created to solve this problem directly.

It is created when ETH enters the Polaris bonding curve and burned when users sell back into the curve. This gives Polaris a shared reserve asset with native liquidity, a rising floor, and value capture built into the collateral itself.

pETH is designed to:
- Enable permissionless 1:1 pAsset issuance against a shared collateral base.
- Scale liquidity as the protocol grows without relying on external liquidity providers.
- Capture value into the collateral asset as Polaris adoption grows.
- Support a rising floor that other Polaris mechanisms can build on.
- Turn ETH into collateral that is productive inside Polaris, without being a liquid staked asset.

The bonding curve is what makes this possible. Its power-law design lets pETH scale with demand while keeping liquidity native to the protocol.

As more activity flows through Polaris, pETH is designed to become increasingly useful as collateral, with deeper liquidity and stronger support across the ecosystem. The long-term objective is for it to become the most effective ETH-backed collateral in DeFi.

## The Bonding Curve
The Polaris bonding curve lets users buy and sell pETH directly against the protocol.
When users buy pETH, ETH enters the reserve and new pETH is created. When users sell pETH, pETH is burned and ETH leaves the reserve.

## Why It Matters
Most DeFi assets depend on external markets for liquidity. If liquidity providers leave, exit conditions can deteriorate quickly.
pETH works differently. The curve gives pETH a protocol-native path in and out of ETH without the reliance on any external liquidity provider that may decide to leave during times of volatility.

## The pETH Floor
The bonding curve gives pETH a floor price measured in ETH under the curve assumptions.
As protocol activity routes value back into the system, fees and burns are designed to lift that floor over time.

The key concept is:
- pETH can trade above its floor.
- The floor represents the minimum value backed by the bonding curve.
- A higher floor gives pETH stronger reserve backing.
- Split, reserve loans, and pAsset collateral all build on this foundation.
For the full explanation, see [pETH Floor Price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price).

## pETH Beyond pAssets
pETH is purpose-built collateral for Polaris, but it can also become useful across DeFi.

Users can:
- Hold pETH as productive collateral.
- Use pETH to create pAssets.
- Split pETH into fpETH and vpETH.
- Use fpETH in reserve loan strategies.
- Integrate pETH into external lending, liquidity, or structured products over time.

## Risk Overview
pETH carries three main risks:
- Technical risk: the bonding curve is smart-contract infrastructure.
- Execution risk: using the curve depends on Ethereum liveness, gas, slippage, and MEV conditions.
- Price risk: pETH can be volatile against ETH above its floor.

The bonding curve removes external liquidity-provider withdrawal risk. It does not remove all risk.

## pETH Floor Price

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price.md

## What It Is

The pETH floor is the code-enforced minimum price of pETH in ETH terms, enforced by the bonding curve.

It is not a treasury promise, discretionary backstop, or target price. It is a result of the curve's reserves, supply, and burn rules, enforced directly by immutable code while the relevant contract invariants hold.

The easiest way to think about it:
- pETH can and will trade above its floor.
- The floor is the code-enforced lower bound created by the bonding curve.
- Protocol activity is designed to push the floor upward over time.
- Your curve-implied downside in ETH terms depends on the distance between your entry price and the floor.

## The Curve
The Polaris bonding curve prices pETH using a power-law relationship between price and supply:

$$
P = \alpha \cdot Q^{\beta}
$$

Where:
- P is the pETH price in ETH.
- Q is the pETH supply.
- alpha is a scaling parameter.
- beta is the curve exponent.

As pETH supply increases, the curve price rises. As pETH is sold or burned, supply falls.

beta controls how tightly pETH tracks ETH:
- A higher beta makes pETH more sensitive to supply changes.
- A lower beta keeps pETH more closely coupled to ETH.

Current testnet values live in [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters). Public Testnet 1 values are testnet-only. Production values should not be inferred until they are published in Launch Status and Parameters.

## What Moves The Floor
The pETH floor is raised by burning pETH.

When pETH is permanently removed from supply and the ETH released from the curve is routed back into the system, the floor price increases for the remaining pETH.

This means the floor is driven by activity that creates burnable pETH flow, including:
- Conversion volume, where pETH is burned to create POLAR.
- Bonding curve trading fees that accrue as pETH and are later burned.
- Other pETH-denominated protocol fees directed to floor-increasing burns.

The ETH reserve supports the bonding curve, but the floor does not rise simply because more ETH is sitting in the reserve. The floor rises when protocol activity produces pETH burns that permanently reduce supply.

In simple terms: adoption creates activity, activity creates fee flow, fee flow creates burns, and burns raise the floor.

Image: The rising pETH floor as a staircase stepping up over time, each step driven by swap-fee burns, conversion-auction burns, or other pETH-denominated fee burns

## Floor Ratio
The floor ratio expresses the floor as a percentage of the current pETH market price:

Floor ratio = Floor price / Market price * 100

It tells you the curve-implied drawdown to the floor before gas, slippage, MEV, smart-contract risk, and chain-liveness risk.

Floor ratio | Curve-implied drawdown to floor

90% | 10%
67% | 33%
55% | 45%

A higher floor ratio means less curve-implied downside to the floor. A lower floor ratio means more distance between market price and floor.

The floor ratio can move over time:
- In periods of strong demand, the market price can move far above the floor and the ratio can fall.
- As the market price normalizes or the floor catches up, the ratio can rise.
- As the floor ratio rises, remaining downside to the floor becomes smaller.

The floor ratio frames curve-implied downside in ETH terms. It does not include gas, slippage, MEV, smart-contract risk, or Ethereum liveness risk. For the full risk model, see [Risk Disclosure](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/risk-disclosure).

Image: A volatile pETH market-price line above a steadily rising floor line, showing the floor ratio as the distance between them

Use the explorer to see how pETH supply, market price, floor price, and floor ratio move under different curve settings.

## pETH Split: fpETH and vpETH

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split.md

pETH combines two sources of value: its floor value and its premium above the floor.

The Split mechanism separates those two components:
```text
1 pETH = 1 fpETH + 1 vpETH
```

Splitting does not create extra value. It separates one pETH token into two interconnected tokens, so users can hold, trade, merge, or borrow against the part of pETH they want.

## The Two Legs

Token | What it represents | Main use

fpETH | The floor-value component of pETH | Passive ETH yield, reserve loan collateral, looping within DeFi
vpETH | The premium above the floor | Hold or trade the upside and volatility component of pETH

Think of pETH as:
pETH value = floor value + premium above floor
fpETH isolates the floor-value side. vpETH isolates the remaining premium.

If pETH trades close to its floor, vpETH may be worth very little. If pETH trades far above its floor, vpETH carries most of that premium exposure.

## Example
If pETH trades at 1.00 ETH:

Floor price | Floor ratio | fpETH claim (floor leg) | vpETH exposure (premium leg)

0.60 ETH | 60% | ~0.60 ETH | ~0.40 ETH
0.90 ETH | 90% | ~0.90 ETH | ~0.10 ETH

This is only a simple decomposition. fpETH and vpETH can each trade on their own markets, so realized prices may differ.

## Split And Merge
Splitting deposits pETH into the splitter contract and sends equal amounts of fpETH and vpETH to your wallet.

Merging burns equal amounts of fpETH and vpETH and releases the matching pETH.

You need both legs in equal amounts to merge back into pETH. If you sell or deposit one leg, you must reacquire it before merging.

## fpETH Redemption
fpETH can be redeemed through the splitter for WETH based on the current pETH floor price.

That redemption path is why fpETH represents the floor leg and is enforced by the splitter and bonding curve mechanics while the relevant contract invariants hold.

## Reserve Loans
Reserve loans use fpETH as collateral.

The borrowable WETH amount is based on the fpETH amount and the current pETH floor price, not on pETH's market premium.

This reduces liquidation risk from pETH premium swings, because the loan is sized against the floor-value component rather than the volatile premium.

## Non-Staking ETH Yield
fpETH is designed to give users ETH-denominated yield without relying on staking.

Its value is tied to the pETH floor. When Polaris activity raises the floor, the fpETH redemption value rises with it.

That makes fpETH a new kind of ETH yield asset: not staking yield, not lending yield, and not offchain yield. It is yield from protocol activity increasing the floor of pETH.

Because fpETH represents the floor-value component of pETH, it is structurally the lower-risk side of the split. It does not need pETH to trade higher in the market. It only needs the pETH floor to keep rising.

fpETH is not risk-free. It still depends on the splitter, bonding curve, Ethereum execution, and smart-contract correctness. But within the Polaris system, it is designed to be the most conservative way to earn from pETH floor growth.

# POLAR

POLAR is the stewardship and growth token of Polaris.

The core protocol is built to function through pETH and pAssets. pETH backs the system, pAssets provide decentralized currencies and synthetic assets, and Earn Vaults help absorb liquidations while earning from protocol activity.

POLAR exists for a different purpose: coordination, growth, and long-term stewardship.

## Why POLAR Exists

Polaris separates the protocol core from the token used to grow and coordinate the
ecosystem.

The core protocol should not depend on emissions to survive. pAssets can be created, the bonding curve can operate, and Earn Vaults can earn from protocol activity without relying on POLAR incentives.

POLAR exists to help Polaris scale beyond the core.

It can support:
- External liquidity.
- Ecosystem incentives.
- Flow onboarding.
- Protocol integrations.
- Future stewardship through vePOLAR.

## Created By Burning pETH

After the initial distribution, new POLAR is intended to enter through one primary path: converting pETH into POLAR.

This conversion is one-way and cannot be reversed.

When users convert pETH into POLAR, pETH is burned. Burning pETH reduces supply, generates yield that is directed via the pETH flows and helps raise the pETH floor for remaining pETH holders.

That means POLAR issuance is tied to activity that strengthens the collateral base, rather than a fixed emissions schedule disconnected from protocol usage.

For the full mechanism, see [Conversion Auctions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions).

## Productive Dilution

POLAR issuance is dilution, but it is designed to be productive dilution.

New POLAR is created when pETH is burned. That burn raises the pETH floor and improves the reserve foundation that supports Polaris.

The goal is not to emit tokens for their own sake, but to exchange pETH burns for ecosystem growth, liquidity, and stewardship alignment. It also captures demand for POLAR and converts it into yield and value capture for the broader protocol.

## vePOLAR Stewardship

POLAR's long-term role is stewardship through vePOLAR.

vePOLAR is the forthcoming vote-escrow layer for Polaris. Users will lock POLAR to receive stewardship rights and participate in bounded protocol coordination.

The goal is not open-ended governance over the core protocol. The core remains constrained. vePOLAR is designed to coordinate parameters, incentives, integrations, and ecosystem growth within defined limits.

Current testnet staking surfaces are temporary and should not be treated as part of the final POLAR design.

Do not use a conversion, staking, or locking interface unless [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) and [Contracts And Addresses](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#contracts-and-addresses) list it as live.

## Stewardship Without Mutable Core Logic

POLAR is designed to coordinate the ecosystem without making the core protocol dependent on open-ended governance.

The core remains constrained. vePOLAR stewardship is intended to coordinate parameters, incentives, integrations, and ecosystem growth within defined limits.

This gives Polaris a path to adapt and scale while preserving the immutability of the base system.

## POLAR Conversion Auctions

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions.md

Conversion auctions are the mechanism that creates new POLAR after the initial distribution.

Converters burn pETH and receive newly created POLAR. Burning pETH reduces supply, helps raise the pETH floor, and releases ETH from the bonding curve that can be routed back into the system.

This makes POLAR issuance directly connected to pETH floor growth and protocol activity, not a fixed emissions schedule.

Image: A converter burns pETH, receives new POLAR, raises the pETH floor, and releases ETH from the bonding-curve reserve

## What Conversion Does

Each conversion has three effects:
- pETH is permanently burned.
- New POLAR is issued to the converter.
- ETH released from the bonding curve reserve can be routed as rebate and system yield.

Conversions are one-way. POLAR cannot be converted back into pETH through the protocol.

## Spike-And-Decay Pricing

Conversion runs as a continuous, permissionless auction.

Anyone can convert pETH into POLAR at any time. Whether it is profitable depends on the conversion price, the POLAR market price, and any rebate available at the time.

The auction price follows a spike-and-decay model:
1. The conversion price decays while nobody converts.
2. When the price falls low enough, conversion become attractive.
3. When a conversion happens, the price spikes upward.
4. After the spike, decay resumes until the next conversion.

The spike is based on how much POLAR supply is created. A large conversion spikes the price more than a small conversion, which makes large one-shot conversions less efficient.

This steers the system toward smaller, more frequent conversions instead of sudden, unbounded issuance.

Image: Conversion price decays until it reaches an arbitrage window, then spikes after conversion and begins decaying again

## Why Burning pETH Matters

Burning pETH is what makes POLAR issuance productive.

When pETH is burned, supply is permanently reduced. Under the bonding curve design, that raises the floor price for the remaining pETH.

This means POLAR creation is tied to a structural improvement in the collateral base. Existing POLAR holders are diluted, but the system receives a pETH burn and potential floor growth in return.

For the full floor mechanism, see [pETH Floor Price](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/floor-price).

## Converter Rebate

Before released ETH becomes system yield, a portion can be returned to the converter as a rebate.

The rebate is a stewardship-controlled dial between conversion volume and system yield.

The rebate changes how released ETH is distributed. It does not change the underlying pETH burn.

After any rebate, remaining ETH is swapped back into pETH and routed through the [Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router) to support pETH yield flows.

## Who Converts

Conversion is permissionless, but it is primarily designed for arbitrageurs, keepers, and MEV searchers.

Most users will not need to convert manually. They will typically buy POLAR off the market where they usually swap tokens then hold POLAR or lock it as vePOLAR.

Manual conversion only makes sense when the quoted POLAR output plus any rebate is more attractive than buying POLAR in the open market.

## How To Convert

1. Open the official conversion interface when Launch Status lists it as live.
2. Review the conversion price, POLAR market price, rebate, and expected output.
3. Enter the amount of pETH to convert.
4. Check the resulting price spike and final quote.
5. Convert only if the quote is better than the open market alternative.
6. Sign the transaction.
7. Receive POLAR and any rebate shown in the quote.

On Public Testnet 1, conversions are Sepolia-only where exposed. Production conversions are not actionable until [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) publishes the official app, contracts, final parameters, and audit status.

## Peg Defense: Issuance And Redemptions

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations.md

Polaris uses two permissionless mechanisms to help pAssets trade around their reference value.

When a pAsset trades above peg, new issuance can expand supply.

When a pAsset trades below peg, redemptions can contract supply.

Together, these mechanisms create arbitrage loops that push pAssets back toward peg without relying on an offchain issuer or discretionary intervention.

## When pAssets Trade Above Peg

If USDp trades above $1, an arbitrageur can create new USDp against pETH collateral and sell it into the market.

That increases USDp supply and applies downward pressure on price.

In simple terms:
1. USDp trades above peg.
2. An arbitrageur adds pETH collateral and mints USDp.
3. The arbitrageur sells USDp into the market to capture the spread.
4. Supply expands until the arbitrage opportunity closes.

## When pAssets Trade Below Peg

If USDp trades below $1, an arbitrageur can buy USDp cheaply and redeem it for pETH collateral at the protocol reference value, minus fees and execution costs.

That buys USDp from the market and reduces supply.

In simple terms:
1. USDp trades below peg.
2. An arbitrageur buys discounted USDp.
3. The arbitrageur redeems it for pETH collateral.
4. Supply contracts until the opportunity closes.

> **Note:** Redemptions assume the system is fully backed. If the protocol becomes undercollateralized — its reserve-to-debt ratio drops below 100% — the collateral returned on redemption is scaled down in proportion, on top of fees. Confirm the quoted collateral in the app before signing.

## How Positions Are Affected

Issuance and redemptions are socialized across all open positions for the affected pAsset.

No position is targeted before another. The protocol does not redeem against the riskiest position first, and a low LTV does not exempt a position from redemption exposure.

New issuance adds collateral and debt across open positions. Redemptions reduce collateral and debt across open positions.

Redemptions are not liquidations. If your position is redeemed against, your debt and collateral can both shrink. Your LTV may improve, but your position becomes smaller and your pETH exposure falls.

Issuance and redemption fees also flow to the affected positions. Unlike designs where these fees are routed to a separate stakeholder group, Polaris directs spike-and-decay fees back to the positions impacted by the mechanism.

## Why This Matters

Polaris does not rely on an offchain issuer to defend pAsset pegs.

Above-peg pressure invites issuance.

Below-peg pressure invites redemptions.

Both mechanisms are permissionless and driven by arbitrage. The protocol uses this activity as a peg signal for autonomous interest rates.

## What To Watch

- Fees, gas, slippage, and MEV affect whether arbitrage is profitable.
- Issuance can increase the size of open positions by adding debt and collateral.
- Redemptions can shrink open positions even when they are solvent.
- Lower LTV protects against liquidation, not redemption.
- Issuance and redemption activity can change system-wide debt and collateral.

## Polaris Liquidations

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations.md

A liquidation closes a position when its LTV rises above the maximum.

Liquidations protect protocol solvency and help ensure pAssets remain overcollateralized. Every pAsset is backed by collateral, and positions that cross the maximum LTV must be closed to keep the system healthy.

A liquidation is different from a redemption. Liquidations close unsafe positions to protect solvency. Redemptions are peg-defense actions that can affect solvent positions when a pAsset trades below peg.

## What Happens

When a position is liquidated:
1. The position's debt is cancelled.
2. Its pETH collateral is seized.
3. The debt is absorbed by the Earn Vault if possible.
4. If the Earn Vault cannot absorb it, the remaining debt and collateral are redistributed across other positions.

## Earn Vault First

The Earn Vault is the first line of defense.

If the Earn Vault has enough pAsset liquidity, it burns pAssets equal to the liquidated debt and receives the liquidated pETH collateral.

Because positions are normally liquidated while still overcollateralized, the Earn Vault can receive more value in pETH than the pAssets it burns. That surplus is part of the yield earned by depositors.

## Redistribution Fallback

If the Earn Vault is empty or too small, liquidation falls back to redistribution.

In redistribution, the liquidated debt and collateral are spread across the remaining positions for that pAsset. Other positions take on a proportional share of both debt and pETH collateral.

## Position Owner Impact

Liquidation is involuntary.

A liquidated position gives up collateral to cover:
- Debt.
- Liquidation penalty.
- Gas compensation or keeper incentive where applicable.

Any remaining surplus can be claimed by the former borrower where supported by the contracts.

The best defense is simple: keep your LTV comfortably below the maximum.

## What To Watch

- Liquidation can happen quickly during volatile markets.
- Thin LTV buffers leave little room for price movement.
- Oracle prices, execution timing, gas, and keeper availability matter.
- Earn Vault depositors earn by absorbing liquidation risk.
- If the Earn Vault is insufficient, redistribution can affect other positions.

## Recovery Mode

Recovery Mode does not change the liquidation line.

A position is liquidatable when its LTV exceeds the normal maximum, in every mode.

Recovery Mode only restricts borrower actions that would make system backing weaker.

## Recovery Mode

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode.md

Recovery Mode is the protective state Polaris enters when system backing becomes thin relative to total pAsset debt.

It is not a shutdown and it does not freeze the system. It restricts actions that would make system health worse.

## The System Health Metric

Each pAsset market has a system-wide health metric that compares reserve backing to total debt.

When that ratio falls below the Recovery Mode threshold, the market enters Recovery Mode automatically.

Current thresholds live in [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

## What Changes

Recovery Mode does not change which positions are liquidatable.

A position is still liquidated only when its LTV exceeds the normal maximum.

What changes is the set of actions issuers can take.

Allowed actions:
- Add collateral.
- Repay debt.
- Make adjustments that improve or preserve system backing.

Restricted actions:
- Withdraw collateral.
- Issue more debt.
- Make adjustments that increase LTV or weaken backing.

In short: actions that make the system safer remain open. Actions that make the system riskier face tighter rules.

Recovery Mode does not automatically close healthy positions and does not change the normal liquidation threshold.

## How To Stay Safe

The defense is the same in every mode: keep a healthy LTV buffer.

To reduce risk:
1. Add pETH collateral.
2. Repay pAsset debt.
3. Avoid running positions close to the maximum LTV.
4. Build your buffer before volatile conditions arrive.

Recovery Mode is most likely during market stress, when adding collateral may be harder and execution may be more expensive.

## Polaris Stewardship, Not Governance

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship.md

Governance risk comes from scope.

When token holders can vote on anything, undefined boundaries and misaligned incentives create capture risk.

Polaris takes a different path: an immutable core with a clearly scoped stewardship layer for the decisions that cannot or should not be fully automated.

Calling it governance would be misleading. Polaris does not give token holders open-ended control over the protocol. It gives stewards a bounded role around the parts of the system that still require human judgment.

Current status:
Stewardship is not live yet. POLAR and vePOLAR contract addresses, proposal thresholds, voting periods, execution delays, and final numeric parameter bounds await audited deployment. These docs describe the intended scope and lifecycle without inventing launch parameters.

## The Core Is Immutable

The mechanisms that define Polaris cannot be changed by anyone: not the team, not vePOLAR, and not a vote.

They are enforced by immutable contracts:
- The bonding curve backing pETH.
- pAsset market logic and position accounting.
- Liquidations.
- Redemptions.
- Earn Vault mechanics.

The base protocol does not need to be relearned after each vote, because the core logic is out of reach by design.

## What Stewardship Controls

Stewardship operates at the edges.

It can coordinate onboarding decisions, bounded parameter dials, Flow participation, fee and interest splits, and treasury programs.

Safety-critical adjustable values ship with hardcoded limits. The remaining numeric bounds are set at audited deployment.

pAsset market onboarding:
Stewardship can approve a team or pAsset market to deploy a new pAsset on Polaris infrastructure.
Constraint: standardized eligibility and launch requirements.

pAsset market parameters:
Stewardship can adjust permitted fee and distribution settings for a pAsset market.
Constraint: hardcoded minimum and maximum bounds.

Conversion parameters:
Stewardship can tune pETH-to-POLAR conversion settings such as rebate, half-life, and spike sensitivity.
Constraint: rebate is bounded between 0% and 100%; other bounds are pending final deployment.

Flow onboarding:
Stewardship can whitelist eligible contracts into Flow revenue sharing.
Constraint: recipients must build on pETH or pAssets and meet published requirements.

Primary interest split:
Stewardship can set how issuer interest is split between the Earn Vault, vePOLAR, and ecosystem paths.
Constraint: final bounds are pending deployment.

Flow weights:
Stewardship can set whitelisted recipient weights and related edge parameters.
Constraint: hardcoded limits. No gauges, bribes, or POLAR emissions.

Treasury programs:
Stewardship can operate programs such as the Polar Council.
Constraint: framework, eligibility, allocation, and reporting requirements are pending publication.

A stewardship vote can bring a new pAsset or Flow participant into the system, or move an approved parameter within its range.

It cannot alter the bonding curve, redemption mechanism, liquidation logic, Earn Vault mechanics, or core contract immutability.

## What Stewardship Does Not Do

Polaris stewardship does not:
- Upgrade or replace immutable core contracts.
- Add admin keys, pause powers, freeze powers, or blacklists.
- Create POLAR emissions gauges.
- Route bribes or vote-buying markets through the protocol.
- Create POLAR outside the pETH conversion mechanism.
- Move safety-critical parameters outside their hardcoded bounds.
- Change a user's position, Earn Vault deposit, pETH balance, POLAR balance, or vePOLAR lock by discretion.

## Operator Prerequisites

Teams seeking onboarding, a pAsset market, Flow participation, or another stewarded action should expect to provide:
- Clear contract addresses once deployed and verified.
- A concise description of the product, risk surface, and user flow.
- The exact stewarded action requested.
- Requested parameter values, with published min and max bounds shown beside them.
- Evidence that the action cannot touch immutable core logic.
- Security status: audits, tests, admin roles, upgradeability, and emergency controls for the requesting team's own contracts.
- Revenue-sharing, Flow, or integration assumptions, if relevant.
- Monitoring and reporting commitments after approval.

The final submission template is pending. Until it is published, operators should prepare around these fields rather than assume an informal path.

## Proposal And Voting Lifecycle

The final stewardship UI and contract-level process are pending.

The intended lifecycle is:

Draft:
Describe the request, affected contracts, user impact, risks, and requested parameters.

Bounds check:
Confirm each requested value sits inside immutable minimum and maximum limits.

Security review:
Confirm verified source, audit status, admin roles, and integration risks.

Vote:
vePOLAR holders vote according to the published lock and vote-power rules.

Queue and execution:
Approved changes are queued and executed through the published contract path.

Verification:
Docs, indexers, and monitors confirm the new state onchain.

Monitoring:
Operators and stewards watch the result and propose reversals or adjustments if needed.

No proposal is executable until the official process, contracts, and parameter bounds are published.

## The Three-Tier Decision Model

Polaris follows a simple ordering: solve a problem at the lowest tier that can solve it, and escalate only what the tier below cannot handle.

1. Automate what can be automated.
Interest-rate setting, floor-price growth, liquidation processing, and peg defence run autonomously.

2. Incentivize what cannot be automated.
Where behavior cannot be hardcoded but can be paid for, durable protocol revenue and Flow formulas handle it.

3. Steward only what is left.
Only decisions that require judgment go to vePOLAR.

## Bounded Blast Radius

Stewards hold real power, so they can make real mistakes.

The immutable core caps the damage.

A weak pAsset affects that market in isolation, not the bonding curve or liquidation engine. A poor split can be moved back inside the same hardcoded bounds.

The lesson Polaris draws is simple: bound the scope first. Less can be captured, overpaid for, or changed away from the base design.

## Fee Router And Flows

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router.md

This page covers two related systems:
- Fee Router: internal revenue plumbing for protocol yield.
- Flows: formula-based revenue sharing for approved integrations.

Current status:
Fee Router addresses, ABIs, events, distribution cadence, Flow whitelists, and final parameter bounds are pending audited deployment. Use [Contracts And Addresses](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#contracts-and-addresses) and [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters) for published values.

## Fee Router

The Fee Router receives protocol revenue, buffers it, and sends it to the configured recipient path.

It moves existing assets only. It does not create POLAR, pETH or any other pAsset.

## Inputs

Conversion auctions:
What enters: pETH purchased with released ETH, after any converter rebate.

Bonding-curve swap fees:
What enters: pETH fees from ETH to pETH and pETH to ETH swaps.

These are the only two sources wired into the Fee Router today.

Issuer interest follows a separate pAsset-denominated path. See [Yield Sources](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources).

## Swap Fee Burn And Repurchase

Swap fees take one extra step before distribution.

Accumulated fee pETH is burned at the floor price, which lifts the pETH floor and releases reserve ETH.

That ETH is then used to buy pETH on the bonding curve at the current curve price and distributed through the Fee Router.

The burn is the floor-raising mechanism. What reaches destinations is repurchased pETH, not the burned fees themselves.

Anyone can trigger the burn-and-repurchase step. Sending buffered pETH onward to a destination is a stewardship action.

## Outputs

Positions:
Receive pETH yield credited proportionally to recorded debt.

Flow recipients:
Receive revenue-sharing distributions for whitelisted contracts.

The Fee Router buffers incoming value before distribution. Buffering smooths bursty conversions and swap fees into steadier accounting periods.

The converter rebate sits upstream of the router. It controls how much released ETH returns to the converter before the rest becomes system yield. See [Conversion Auctions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/polar/conversion-auctions).

## Operator Checks

Before relying on the Fee Router, operators and indexers should verify:
- Router address, source, and ABI.
- Published input sources.
- Current recipient path and any minimum or maximum bounds.
- Distribution cadence and claim path.
- Events for deposits, buffering, distributions, claims, and parameter changes.
- Audit notes specific to router accounting.

Indexers should track inputs by source, buffered amounts before distribution, each distribution or claim, and the parameter values active during the distribution.

## Flows

Flows share protocol revenue with approved contracts that create durable demand for Polaris assets.

They are designed to replace recurring token emissions with revenue paid in pETH or pAssets.

The base rule:

Flow share = contract's weighted asset held / total weighted asset held across whitelisted contracts

If a whitelisted contract holds 20% of the whitelisted pETH supply, it receives 20% of that Flow before launch-specific adjustments.

## Flow Families

pETH Flow:
Recipients: eligible pETH-holding contracts.
Funded by: bonding-curve and conversion-related revenue.

pAsset Flow:
Recipients: eligible pAsset-holding contracts.
Funded by: issuer interest or other pAsset revenue after required Earn Vault allocation.

Each Flow has its own whitelist, observed asset, recipient rules, and parameter bounds.

Final contracts will define which assets, recipients, and revenue sources go live.

## Stewarded Edges

The Flow formula should run automatically once configured.

Stewardship is limited to edge decisions:
- Admit or remove a contract from a whitelist.
- Set a bounded weight or kickstart where allowed.
- Distinguish observed address from recipient address where contracts support it.
- Adjust entries inside published bounds.

Flow eligibility is not automatic.

Operators should prepare product details, verified contracts, admin and upgrade model, requested Flow, monitoring plan, and user risk disclosure.

## Flow Lifecycle

Application:
Operator submits product, contracts, requested Flow, and risk details.

Review:
Stewards check eligibility, security posture, and bounds.

Vote:
vePOLAR votes on whitelist admission and settings.

Activation:
Recipient is added through the official execution path.

Distribution:
Formula allocates revenue by holdings, weights, and active parameters.

Monitoring:
Holdings, distributions, and risks are tracked.

## Flows Versus Emissions

Emission gauges:
Currency: governance token emissions.
Funding: inflation.
Allocation: recurring votes and bribes.
Stewardship load: ongoing weight wars.

Polaris Flows:
Currency: pETH or pAssets.
Funding: protocol revenue.
Allocation: formula based on eligible holdings.
Stewardship load: onboarding plus bounded adjustments.

## Why Polaris

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/why-polaris
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/why-polaris.md

Blockchains gave the world something new: a shared global state that no single institution controls.

DeFi was meant to build on that foundation. A financial layer that is more open and transparent, where participation depends less on trust and more on verifiable rules.

But over time, much of DeFi has drifted back toward the dependencies it was built to escape: custodians, centralized exchanges, multisigs, upgradeable contracts, offchain collateral, and black-box yield.

The result is a financial system that looks onchain from the outside, but often depends on centralized promises underneath.

Users are asked to trust intermediaries they cannot see, legal agreements they cannot verify, and yield sources they do not control. They inherit new risks, but are rarely compensated for them.
Polaris takes a different path.
Built around an immutable foundation, pETH is a yield bearing asset backed by native ETH and designed to support decentralized currencies and synthetic assets without introducing external counterparties or offchain dependencies.
## The Problem
DeFi’s yield economy has become increasingly concentrated around two sources: T-bills and RWAs for stablecoins, and ETH staking for ETH-denominated assets.

Both have their place, but this is not the end state we aim for.
When stablecoin yield depends on RWAs, the system becomes reliant on external institutions to generate and distribute returns. When ETH yield comes primarily from staking, every ETH-denominated asset ends up competing for the same source of return, one that has steadily compressed over time.
This creates a structural dependency problem that worsens as the system grows. DeFi may become more productive on the surface, but underneath it is more reliant on custodians, offchain agreements, upgrade keys, and opaque
operational processes.

Users often do not see these dependencies. They see an APY, a token balance, and an interface that feels onchain, while the systems supporting that yield often rely on assets that can be frozen or contracts that can be upgraded without their consent.

That is not enough for the next era of internet-native finance.

## The Polaris Thesis
The internet needs financial infrastructure that can support both human and machine economies without relying on trusted counterparties.
That requires permissionless money and collateral that remain sovereign throughout their lifecycle, from the assets that back them to the mechanisms that generate their yield.
Polaris is designed around that belief.
The protocol transforms ETH into pETH, a yield-bearing reserve asset with a natively growing floor. pETH can then support the creation of pAssets such as USDp and GOLDp, creating decentralized currencies and synthetic assets that scale with protocol activity.
Instead of importing yield from centralized counterparties, Polaris routes value from its own mechanisms back into the system.
Users can hold pETH as productive collateral, issue pAssets against it, deposit pAssets into Earn Vaults, split pETH into floor and volatility components, and participate in stewardship (our variant of what governance should be) via the POLAR token.
## Design Choices

Choice | Why it exists | What it enables

pETH as common collateral | Gives every pAsset market the same ETH-backed reserve foundation | USDp, GOLDp, and future pAssets can scale from the same collateral reserve, making risk easier to understand across the system
Two-way bonding curve | Gives pETH native liquidity without depending on external liquidity providers | ETH can enter and exit through transparent onchain rules while fees and burns can raise the pETH floor.
Earn Vaults | Turn liquidation and borrower activity into yield opportunities | pAsset holders can earn yield while providing an additional layer of resilience to the system.
POLAR stewardship | Allows decision-making within predefined boundaries while keeping the core logic immutable. | Long-term coordination without rewriting the base protocol
pETH split | Separates floor exposure from upside exposure | Users can choose different risk and return profiles
Direct pAsset issuance | Lets users create pAssets directly from the protocol against pETH collateral 1:1 | New pAssets can scale from native demand without relying on leverage or borrowers
Redemptions | Guarantees pAsset peg stability | Below-peg pressure can be corrected without offchain redemption promises
Peg-responsive interest rates | Adjust borrowing costs based on each pAsset’s peg conditions | Helps balance supply and demand while routing value back to Earn Vaults and POLAR stewardship

## The End Goal
Polaris exists to help DeFi become less dependent on the centralized financial system.
A way to create money that remains decentralized and permissionless, without sacrificing the openness and transparency that blockchains were designed to provide.
A fully censorship-resistant stablecoin may be the product people notice first, but the larger vision is a decentralized yield layer for onchain money.

## Polaris Earn Vaults

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield.md

Earn Vaults are the main pAsset yield venue in Polaris.

Depositors provide pAssets that help process liquidations. In return, they can earn from issuer interest and liquidation gains.

Earn Vaults are first-loss backstops. When a position is liquidated, deposited pAssets can be burned to cover debt and replaced by pETH collateral.

## Why Use It

- Earn pAsset-denominated interest from issuers.
- Receive pETH liquidation gains when liquidations are processed.
- Put idle pAssets to work without opening a position.
- Help keep pAssets backed during stress.

Image: ETH enters the bonding curve as pETH, pETH backs pAsset positions, and pAssets deposited in Earn Vaults absorb liquidated debt and earn discounted pETH plus issuer interest

## How Yield Works

Depositors earn from two streams.

Issuer interest:
A share of the interest paid by positions that issued that pAsset.

Liquidation gains:
When a position is liquidated, the vault can absorb debt and receive pETH collateral. The collateral value is usually higher than the absorbed debt, but realized gains depend on execution and market movement.

Current split values live in [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

## Basic Flow

You need a pAsset to deposit, usually USDp or GOLDp, plus ETH for gas.

1. Open Earn in the Polaris app.
2. Choose the USDp or GOLDp Earn Vault.
3. Enter the deposit amount.
4. Review expected wallet changes.
5. Approve the pAsset if prompted.
6. Sign the deposit transaction.
7. Confirm the Earn Vault deposit appears in the app.

## What To Watch

- Your pAsset deposit can be partially consumed during liquidations.
- Liquidation gains arrive as pETH, which has price risk.
- APR is an estimate, not a guarantee.
- Extreme market moves can create losses even when the mechanism works as intended.
- Earn Vault yield depends on issuer interest, liquidation activity, execution, and market conditions.

## Strategies

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/strategies
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/strategies.md

Polaris is built from a few composable parts - pETH as collateral, pAsset positions, and Earn Vaults. Those parts combine into several common position shapes. This page describes what is possible, not what you should do. Nothing here is advice, and every shape below carries the risks set out in [Risk Disclosure](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/risk-disclosure).

## Hold pETH, no debt

The simplest position is to hold [pETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) with nothing issued against it. pETH is a productive reserve asset with a floor designed to rise as protocol activity routes value back into the curve.

- What it gives: exposure to pETH with no debt and no liquidation risk.
- Main risk: pETH can move against ETH above its floor. There is no peg and no protection beyond the curve floor.

## Issue a pAsset against pETH

Deposit pETH and [issue](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting) a pAsset such as USDp or GOLDp. You keep your pETH collateral, and its ETH exposure, while holding a pegged asset you can use elsewhere.

- What it gives: a pegged asset, dollar or gold, drawn against pETH while retaining collateral upside.
- Main risk: the position has an LTV and a liquidation price, and it accrues [interest](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/interest-rates). Issuance and redemptions can resize it.

## Draw stable value without selling ETH exposure

Because a position lets you issue a USD-pegged pAsset against pETH, you can raise stable liquidity without selling the ETH exposure held in your collateral. Repaying the pAsset later unwinds the position and restores it.

- What it gives: stable liquidity now while keeping collateral exposure for later.
- Main risk: this is leverage. A falling pETH collateral value raises your LTV toward liquidation, and interest accrues the whole time.

## Earn on idle pAssets

Deposit USDp or GOLDp into an [Earn Vault](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield) to earn issuer interest and liquidation gains. The vault is the system's first-loss backstop, so deposits can be consumed during liquidations.

- What it gives: pAsset-denominated yield on otherwise idle pAssets.
- Main risk: first-loss exposure. A deposit can be partially consumed, and liquidation gains arrive as pETH, which has price risk.

## Split pETH and reserve loans

pETH can be [split](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split) into fpETH and vpETH, separating floor value from upside. fpETH can then be used in reserve-loan strategies.

- What it gives: finer control over which part of pETH's value you hold or borrow against.
- Main risk: these are advanced mechanics with their own execution and price risks. Read the linked pages before using them.

## Before acting

These shapes can be combined, and each adds risk as it adds flexibility. None is risk-free, and none is a recommendation. Read [Risk Disclosure](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/risk-disclosure) in full, and confirm current thresholds and fees in [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).

## Polaris Yield Sources

URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources
Markdown URL: https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/yield/yield-sources.md

Polaris yield comes from onchain protocol activity: issuance, liquidations, swaps, and conversions.

This page lists the sources. [Fee Router And Flows](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router) covers routing.

Adoption creates activity. Activity creates fees. Fees create yield. Yield creates more demand.

## The Sources

Issuer interest:
Origin: interest paid by positions that issued pAssets.
Flows to: split by the interest router between Earn Vault depositors, vePOLAR lockers, and other configured destinations.

Liquidation gains:
Origin: penalties and discounted collateral from liquidated positions.
Flows to: Earn Vault depositors.

Bonding curve swap fees:
Origin: fees on ETH to pETH and pETH to ETH swaps.
Flows to: pETH floor growth and positions that receive routed pETH rewards.

Conversion gains:
Origin: ETH released when pETH is burned to create POLAR in a conversion auction.
Flows to: the Fee Router, which recycles value into pETH and routes it to configured destinations.

## What Each Source Supports

Earn Vault yield:
Driven mainly by issuer interest and liquidation gains.

pETH yield:
Driven by bonding curve swap fees, conversion gains, floor growth, and routed pETH rewards.

vePOLAR value capture:
Driven by a guaranteed minimum share of issuer interest from deployed pAsset markets, once vePOLAR is live.

Flow revenue:
Driven by protocol revenue shared with approved integrations that create durable demand for Polaris assets.

## Why This Is Self-Scaling

The primary yield sources depend on protocol activity:
- More pAssets issued can mean more issuer interest.
- More trading can mean more bonding curve swap fees.
- More POLAR created through conversion can mean more conversion gains.
- More integrations can mean more demand for pETH and pAssets.

Liquidations can also create Earn Vault yield, but they exist to protect solvency, not to generate yield for its own sake. They are expected to matter most during market stress, when issuer interest may fall and the system needs backstop liquidity most.

## Owned, Not Rented

Polaris yield is not subsidized, bridged in, or voted into being.

It emerges from protocol use and is captured by the protocol itself.

That makes yield a function of Polaris activity rather than an external incentive budget.

## How Yield Is Distributed

Conversion proceeds and swap fees go through the [Fee Router](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/stewardship/fee-router).

Issuer interest follows separate pAsset-denominated split logic.

Earn Vaults, vePOLAR, positions, and approved Flow recipients each receive yield through their configured path.

Current values and pending production status live in [Parameters](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/resources/testnet#parameters).
