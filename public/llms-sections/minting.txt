# Minting Polaris pAssets

## The CDP model

Polaris issues pAssets through a **Collateralized Debt Position (CDP)**. From the outside it looks like borrowing against collateral, but underneath it differs from a money market like Aave.

- A **money market** matches lenders and borrowers through a shared pool. You borrow funds another user supplied; an interest rate floats with pool utilization.
- A **CDP** lets each borrower **mint** the pAsset directly from the protocol. You aren't borrowing anyone's deposit; you're your own mini central bank: post collateral ⇒ mint a pAsset ⇒ owe that debt back.

Each position is isolated in a **[trove](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove)** with its own collateral, debt, and collateralization ratio. Users of a given pAsset share only (1) the pAsset and its peg state, and (2) system-wide parameters like the [Total Collateralization Ratio and Recovery Mode](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode). That isolation bounds the blast radius: each pAsset keeps its own debt accounting, so trouble in one cannot reach the troves or collateral of another.

## Borrower terminology

Term you may see | Docs term | Meaning

**Borrow** | User action | The app tab/action for taking on debt.
**CDP** | Mechanism | The collateralized debt contract model for a pAsset instance.
**Trove** | Position | Your individual pAsset borrowing position: collateral, debt, and ICR.
**Loan / Reserve Loan** | Separate product | A WETH loan against fpETH floor-value collateral, not a pAsset trove.

When these docs say **trove**, they mean a pAsset debt position. When the app says **Reserve Loan**, read [pETH Split: fpETH and vpETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth/split) instead; reserve loans use fpETH, not pETH trove collateral.

### Strengths and trade-offs

The CDP model is ~8 years proven, its strengths and weaknesses well understood; Polaris's design amplifies the former and dampens the latter.

Strengths | Trade-offs

**Trustless** — logic fully enforced onchain, no intermediaries | **Liquidation risk** — collateral below threshold can be [liquidated](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations)
**Transparent** — ratios and liquidation logic auditable onchain | **Volatility** — sharp collateral drops can trigger liquidations
**Optionality** — unlock liquidity without selling your assets | **Smart-contract risk** — mitigated by audits, never zero
**Debt-asset agnostic** — the minted asset can be anything | **Capital inefficiency** — overcollateralization leaves capital idle

## What Polaris does differently

### Collateral is pETH, and only pETH

Polaris does not accept wstETH or other staked-ETH tokens: they would be external dependencies that cap scaling. It accepts only **[pETH](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth)**, ETH deposited into the bonding curve. This dampens the CDP model's capital-inefficiency weakness, because pETH is:

1. an ETH proxy with a floor price designed to rise under the curve assumptions, and
2. **yield-bearing** — collateral pETH also earns from other parts of the system (e.g. ETH released by conversions).

### Autonomous, market-aware interest rates

Setting CDP interest rates is where most designs struggle: Maker still uses governance-set rates (spiky, unpredictable); Liquity v1 had none (sustained supply pressure as T-bill rates rose); Liquity v2 pushes rate-setting onto users.

Polaris sets rates **autonomously, without a direct market-price feed for the pAsset**, by reading signals already inside the system:

- **Redemptions are profitable when pUSD < \$1** ⇒ a spike in redemption volume signals a downward depeg ⇒ raise the rate.
- **Minting is profitable when pUSD > \$1** ⇒ a spike in minting volume signals an upward depeg ⇒ lower the rate.

This gives the protocol an internal peg-pressure signal, not a substitute for every oracle in the system. Collateral valuation, non-dollar pAsset reference units, stale-price checks, and liquidation math still depend on the final audited pricing paths for those assets. See [Redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations) for the mechanism behind the peg-pressure signal.

# Managing a Polaris Trove

A trove is a living position. After you [open it](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/open-a-trove), you can adjust it freely: add collateral, mint more, repay debt, or close it. Everything traces back to one number — your **Individual Collateralization Ratio**.

Trove management is testnet-actionable only on Sepolia with test assets and current manifests. Production management is not mainnet-actionable until Polaris Launch Status publishes the official app, production addresses, final parameters, and audit status. Use live app and contract values over examples on this page.

<strong>Scope:</strong> this page covers pAsset troves/CDPs (shown under <strong>Borrow</strong>), not reserve loans. For the full vocabulary, see [Borrower terminology](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting#borrower-terminology).

## Individual Collateralization Ratio (ICR)

Your **Individual Collateralization Ratio (ICR)** is the value of your trove's collateral divided by the recorded value of its debt:

> ICR = collateral value ÷ debt face value

Collateral value comes from the protocol's collateral pricing path. Debt value is what you owe in that pAsset's reference unit, including accrued interest; it is not the temporary market price at which you might buy the pAsset on a DEX. A trove holding pETH worth \$15,000 against 10,000 pUSD of debt has an ICR of 150%. Higher is safer. Two thresholds matter:

- Below the **minimum ICR**, the trove can be [liquidated](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations).
- [Redemptions](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations) draw collateral from every open trove collectively, pro-rata to its recorded collateral and regardless of ICR — a higher ICR protects against liquidation, not redemption.

Your ICR moves on its own as ETH's price moves — you need not touch the trove for it to drift. Managing the trove is mostly about keeping the ICR where you want it.

## Trove Health Simulator

Minting more or withdrawing collateral lowers your ICR. Drop below the minimum and your trove is liquidated automatically: your collateral covers the debt plus a liquidation penalty, with no margin call, and only the surplus is returned for you to claim. Redemptions can also reach your position while it is still solvent — there is no minimum ICR to cross — shrinking your collateral and pETH exposure proportionally without your consent. See the Risk Disclosure.

## Parameter status

Parameter | Status | Why it matters

## Add collateral

Deposit more pETH into the trove to **raise your ICR** without changing your debt. This is the simplest way to step back from a liquidation or redemption threshold during a drawdown. (Need pETH? Mint it on the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth).)

## Mint more / repay debt

- **Mint more.** If your ICR has room, mint more pAsset against the same collateral. This *lowers* your ICR, so leave a buffer.
- **Repay debt.** Burn pAsset back into the trove to reduce your debt and *raise* your ICR. Repaying is the other lever for recovering a thinning position, and the only way to withdraw collateral.

## Monitor interest accrual

Borrowers pay an [autonomous, market-aware interest rate](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting) that accrues continuously against your trove. Interest adds to your debt over time, so your ICR drifts *down* even if ETH holds flat and you do nothing. Check your accrued debt periodically and repay or top up so accrual does not quietly walk you toward a threshold.

Collateral pETH also *earns* yield while it sits in the trove, partly offsetting the interest cost; see [Minting pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting) for how the effective rate is composed.

### Worked example: interest accrual

Suppose your trove owes 10,000 pUSD and the app quotes an 8% annualized borrow rate. As a rough estimate, before rate changes and compounding:

Period | Interest estimate | New debt

30 days | 10,000 × 8% × 30 / 365 = 65.75 pUSD | 10,065.75 pUSD
90 days | 10,000 × 8% × 90 / 365 = 197.26 pUSD | 10,197.26 pUSD

If your collateral value stays flat at \$15,000, the 30-day estimate moves your ICR from 150% to about 149%. The contract may accrue continuously and the rate may change, so use the app's recorded debt for precise decisions.

## Close the trove

Closing is the reverse of opening (**repay debt ⇒ withdraw collateral**):

1. **Repay the full debt.** Burn enough pAsset to clear the recorded debt, including accrued interest. You must hold (or re-acquire) the pAsset.
2. **Withdraw collateral.** With the debt at zero, withdraw your pETH. The trove is closed.

If you do not hold enough pAsset to repay (for example, you sold it), buy it back on the market first.

## How ICR maps to your exposure

ICR | Liquidation exposure | Redemption exposure

Comfortably high | Minimal — survives large ETH drops | Low — redemptions reduce every trove pro-rata to its collateral, and a large buffer keeps your ICR healthy afterward
Moderate | Survives normal volatility | Moderate — a system-wide redemption shrinks your collateral proportionally and can erode your buffer
Near the minimum | High — a sharp ETH move liquidates you | A system-wide redemption reduces your collateral pro-rata and, on thin margin, can leave you closer to the liquidation line

The takeaway: a buffer is not wasted capital, it is the price of staying in control of your position. Keep enough margin that ordinary volatility and quiet interest accrual cannot force an outcome you did not choose.

## Steps

### Connect your wallet

Open the Polaris app and connect.

### Acquire pETH

Deposit ETH into the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) to receive pETH. pETH is the *only* collateral Polaris accepts. If you already hold pETH, skip this step.

### Deposit pETH as collateral

Choose how much pETH to lock in the trove. This collateral stays in the trove until you withdraw it.

### Choose pAsset and amount

Pick the pAsset (pUSD, pGOLD, …) and the amount of debt to take on. The app shows the resulting **Individual Collateralization Ratio (ICR)** — your collateral value divided by your debt value — before you confirm.

### Confirm

Sign the transaction. The minted pAsset arrives in your wallet; the debt is recorded against your trove.

### Monitor your ICR

Your ICR moves with the price of ETH and with any changes you make. Watch it from the trove dashboard and adjust before it nears a threshold. See [Managing Your Trove](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/managing-your-trove).

## Choosing a safe collateralization buffer

Your ICR is the single number that determines your risk. Two distinct events depend on it:

- **[Liquidation](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations).** If your ICR falls below the minimum, your trove is liquidated and you lose collateral to cover the debt (plus a penalty). A low ICR is most dangerous when ETH drops sharply.
- **[Redemption](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/redemptions-liquidations).** When the pAsset trades below peg, arbitrageurs redeem it for pETH. A redemption applies collectively across all open troves in proportion to their recorded collateral, not to a single lowest-ICR position, so it reduces every trove's collateral and debt together regardless of ICR. Being redeemed against carries no liquidation penalty, but it shrinks your position and your directional exposure without your consent.

A larger buffer above the minimum keeps you clear of liquidation through volatility. Redemptions are different: they reach every open trove proportionally regardless of ICR, so a buffer does not keep you out of them, but a healthy ICR ensures a redemption does not leave you near the liquidation line. The right buffer depends on how volatile you expect ETH to be and how actively you intend to manage the trove — a position you watch daily can run leaner than one you set and forget.

There is no margin call and no grace period. If ETH moves against you faster than you top up, liquidation is automatic. Size your buffer for the worst day you are willing to sit through, not the average one.

## Worked example: ICR buffer

The numbers below are illustrative only. They show the math; they are not launch parameters.

Assume the app displays a 120% minimum ICR for a pUSD trove, and you open with \$18,000 of pETH collateral against 10,000 pUSD of debt:

Step | Collateral value | Debt | ICR

Opening position | \$18,000 | 10,000 pUSD | 180%
After a 20% collateral-value drop | \$14,400 | 10,000 pUSD | 144%
After the same drop plus 200 pUSD of accrued interest | \$14,400 | 10,200 pUSD | 141%

That buffer survives the modeled move. Had the same user opened at 135%, a 20% collateral-value drop would take the trove to about 108%, below the illustrative 120% minimum. The lesson is not that 180% is the right number; it is that your buffer has to cover price moves, interest accrual, gas delays, and the way redemptions reduce every open trove's collateral and debt collectively, pro-rata to collateral and regardless of ICR.

# Polaris Asset Catalog

A **pAsset** is a synthetic debt asset minted from a Polaris CDP against pETH collateral. Each pAsset is overcollateralized, counterparty-free, and redeemable through onchain protocol mechanics under the usual assumptions: functioning contracts, live Ethereum execution, available gas, and valid pricing data. **pETH is different:** it is not minted from a trove; it is the bonding-curve collateral token that backs pAssets. The catalog is open: through [StablecoinOS](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting/passet-catalog#stablecoinos-an-open-catalog), credible teams can add new pAssets, all sharing the same pETH collateral and bonding curve.

> **Current Public Testnet 1 focus:** pUSD and pGOLD are Testnet-active on Sepolia. pCHF, pBigMac, and other catalog ideas are illustrative or future until [Launch Status](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/launch-status) says otherwise.

## Polaris assets

Asset | Tracks | Current status | Notes

**pUSD** | US dollar | **Testnet-active** | The flagship pAsset. Counterparty-free dollar with yield that scales with protocol activity. See [Minting pAssets](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/minting).
**pETH** | ETH | **Testnet-active collateral** | *Special.* Not minted from a trove: it is the collateral itself, produced by the [bonding curve](https://tokenbrice.github.io/polaris-docs/peth) with a floor price designed to rise under the curve assumptions. It backs every other pAsset.
**pGOLD** | 1 ounce of gold | **Testnet-active** | Decentralized gold exposure, backed by pETH rather than bullion. Public Testnet 1 uses Sepolia testnet price-feed plumbing; see the comparison below.

## pGOLD vs centralized gold (XAUT/PAXG)

pGOLD and the incumbent gold tokens both give gold-price exposure, but they are structurally different products.

Property | pGOLD | XAUT / PAXG

Collateral | pETH, fully onchain | Physical gold in custody
Redemption | Permissionless, no protocol-level minimum published yet | Issuer-gated; account requirements and redemption minimums depend on the issuer's current terms
KYC | None | Required to redeem
Physical custody | None — nothing to store or insure | Vaulted bullion, with custody risk
Counterparty | Polaris protocol only | Tether / Paxos as issuer

The trade-off is honest: pGOLD inherits ETH-collateral and smart-contract risk where XAUT/PAXG inherit custodial and issuer risk. What pGOLD removes is the *permissioned* layer: no counterparty can freeze, blacklist, or gate your redemption.

## StablecoinOS: an open catalog

The CDP model is **debt-asset agnostic**: it can mint a synthetic pegged to any asset with robust reference pricing and risk parameters. Most protocols ship one stablecoin and stop. Polaris is built as a *factory* of CDP instances under **StablecoinOS**: one CDP per pAsset, all sharing the same pETH collateral pool and bonding curve.

That shared collateral does double duty. It lets credible teams add a new pAsset (pEUR, pSilver, or something stranger) without bootstrapping a fresh collateral base. And because each instance has its own debt asset, a violent day in one market (a gold spike, say) drives liquidations on that instance while the others barely move. The pETH collateral is mutualized; the risk is not.

The result is a catalog that grows without diluting the safety of what already exists.
