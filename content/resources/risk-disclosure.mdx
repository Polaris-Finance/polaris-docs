# Risk Disclosure

Polaris is trustless, immutable infrastructure. That is a design choice with real costs, and we disclose them before we claim the benefits. Every risk below is paired with the mechanism that mitigates it (*mitigates*, not eliminates). Read this page in full before you mint, deposit, or lock anything.

> **Note:** Nothing here is financial advice. Polaris is pre-launch: the official app URL, contract addresses, final audit reports, and disclosure program are not published in these docs yet. Trustlessness means the protocol will not, and cannot, step in to make you whole. You are responsible for understanding the position you take.

## Smart-contract risk

Polaris lives entirely onchain. Its bonding curve, CDPs, Stability Pool, and conversion auctions are smart contracts, and any smart contract can contain bugs.

**Mitigation.** The intended core is immutable and admin-key-free, which removes upgrade and governance-key attack surface but raises the stakes on getting the code right. Final audit reports are not published in these docs yet; until reports appear on [Audits & Security](/resources/audits-security), treat the protocol as unaudited. Immutability is a double-edged sword: it cannot be tampered with, and it cannot be patched. Residual risk is never zero.

## Collateral and price risk

The only collateral is [pETH](/peth/bonding-curve), which is **volatile against ETH**. pETH trades at a varying premium above its [floor price](/peth/floor-price), and that premium can compress sharply in a drawdown.

**Mitigation.** The [floor ratio](/peth/floor-price) bounds your maximum downside in ETH terms: a 90% floor ratio means at most 10% drawdown, a 67% floor ratio at most 33%. Your risk is decided at *entry*: the spread between your entry price and the floor. The floor only ever rises; it cannot retreat.

> **Warning:** A high floor ratio is not a guarantee against ETH's own volatility. pETH protects you in *ETH terms*. If ETH itself falls against the dollar, your pETH falls with it.

## Oracle risk

Pricing pETH collateral for liquidation purposes requires an ETH price feed. A stale, manipulated, or failed oracle could misprice collateral and trigger wrong liquidations, or fail to trigger correct ones.

**Mitigation.** Peg awareness needs no oracle at all: redemption and minting volume act as the protocol's internal price signal for each pAsset (see [Minting pAssets](/minting/minting-passets)). The oracle dependency is intended to be confined to pricing the *collateral*, ETH. The final provider and feed address are forthcoming; do not treat any oracle address as official until it is listed in [Contracts & Addresses](/resources/contracts).

## Peg and depeg risk

A pAsset can trade away from its peg, below it in a panic or above it in a demand spike. Depegs can be temporary or, under severe stress, sustained.

**Mitigation.** Two opposing arbitrage mechanisms defend the peg structurally:

- **[Redemptions](/redemptions-liquidations/redemptions)** make it profitable to buy the pAsset below peg and redeem it for collateral, pushing the price up toward $1.
- **Minting** makes it profitable to mint and sell the pAsset above peg, pushing the price back down.

Because both are profitable for arbitrageurs the moment the peg slips, they activate without anyone's permission. pUSD is designed as a counterparty-free stablecoin in the same trust-minimized family as LUSD and BOLD, so the peg is not propped up by a redeemable offchain reserve; it is held by onchain incentives.

## Liquidation risk (borrowers)

If your [trove](/minting/minting-passets)'s [Individual Collateralization Ratio (ICR)](/resources/glossary) falls below the minimum, the trove is [liquidated](/redemptions-liquidations/liquidations): you lose your collateral to cover the debt.

**Mitigation.** Liquidation logic is transparent and auditable onchain, with no discretionary calls. You control your ICR: post more collateral, or repay debt, to stay clear of the threshold. pETH's rising floor and yield-bearing nature dampen, but do not remove, the volatility that causes liquidations. Monitor your trove actively, especially in volatile markets.

## Stability Pool first-loss risk (depositors)

Depositing pAssets into the [Stability Pool](/yield/stability-pool) is the primary way to earn yield, but the Pool is the **first-loss buffer** for the system. When a trove is liquidated, your deposited pAssets are burned to cancel its debt, and you receive the liquidated pETH collateral in exchange.

**Mitigation.** Liquidations are designed to occur while the trove is still overcollateralized, so depositors typically receive collateral worth *more* than the pAssets they gave up, and that surplus is the source of the Pool's yield. The risk is the opposite case: a liquidation processed below the value of the burned deposit, or a sharp collapse in the value of the received collateral. You are taking on liquidation processing risk in exchange for yield; size your deposit accordingly.

## Recovery Mode

When the system-wide reserve backing relative to total debt (the [reserve-to-debt ratio, shown as the TCR](/resources/glossary)) drops below a threshold, the protocol enters [Recovery Mode](/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode), a defensive state with tighter requirements.

**Mitigation.** Recovery Mode is a feature, not a failure: it raises the bar for the whole system to rebuild collateralization quickly. The cost is borne by borrowers: while the system is in Recovery Mode you cannot withdraw collateral or take on more debt unless you clear a higher minimum ratio and do not lower your ICR (pure collateral top-ups and repayments stay allowed). Liquidation eligibility itself does not change; it stays at the normal minimum in every mode. Keep your ICR comfortably above the minimum so a stress event does not box in your ability to act.

## Stewardship and economic risk

Stewards control quantitative parameters: the interest splits, conversion-price parameters, and the [converter rebate](/resources/glossary). Parameter changes can affect your yield and the economics of your position.

**Mitigation.** Stewardship is *constrained*: many economic parameters move only within hardcoded ranges (for example the converter rebate and the interest-split floors), and the core logic cannot be touched at all (see [Stewardship, not Governance](/stewardship/stewardship)). Some conversion-price parameters are not yet bounded in the current prototype; instead, conversion issuance is rate-limited by its spike-and-decay pricing, so each conversion raises the price in proportion to the POLAR it mints and large or rapid minting throttles itself. The risk that remains is ordinary parameter risk, not the unbounded-governance risk that has denatured other protocols. Final per-parameter bounds are pending audited deployment.

## MEV risk

Redemptions, liquidations, and [conversion auctions](/polar/conversion-auctions) are profitable, permissionless actions, so they attract MEV searchers and arbitrage bots. Ordinary users competing by hand will usually lose the race to automation.

**Mitigation.** This is by design: MEV competition is what keeps the peg tight and the floor rising, because searchers do the protocol's stabilizing work for profit. As a user, do not expect to win arbitrage races manually; interact with the protocol for the position you want (a trove, a Stability Pool deposit, a pETH hold), not to front-run the bots.

## Dependency risk (Ethereum)

Polaris runs on Ethereum mainnet (chain ID 1) and inherits its risks: consensus failures, severe congestion, or a contentious fork.

**Mitigation.** This dependency is deliberate and unhedged: the only counterparties Polaris is intended to ask you to trust are immutable code and Ethereum itself. We do not spread across chains to dilute that exposure, because doing so would reintroduce the bridge and external-dependency risks Polaris exists to avoid.

## Frontend and phishing risk

Before launch, fake frontends, fake contract addresses, and fake support channels can appear before users know what the official ones look like. A malicious frontend can ask you to approve tokens, sign dangerous messages, or connect to the wrong network.

**Mitigation.** Do not use any app URL, network, chain ID, contract address, audit claim, or bug-bounty link unless it is listed in these docs. Never enter a seed phrase, private key, or recovery words into any Polaris-related site. The current status checklist lives on [Safety and Verification](/paths/safety-verification).
