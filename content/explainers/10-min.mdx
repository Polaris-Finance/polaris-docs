# Polaris 10-Minute Introduction

## Where stablecoins break down

DeFi has had two kinds of stablecoin: centralized-but-scalable and trustless-but-small.

The pattern is structural. A project starts with relatively clean collateral (crypto assets, onchain yield); as it grows, it adds real-world assets, centralized stablecoins, or CEX-dependent yield to keep pace with demand. The economics push that way: T-bills and lending desks are large, reliable, and easy to integrate. Onchain alternatives are harder to scale.

The result: the largest stablecoins in DeFi are managed by entities that can freeze balances, blacklist addresses, and change collateral composition at will. They are useful, but not counterparty-free.

Meanwhile, the trustless designs — LUSD, BOLD — have proven immutable CDPs work. But without a scalable onchain yield source, their growth is constrained, their rates volatile, and they have stayed in a trustless-focused niche.

The open question is whether a stablecoin can be *both* trustless and scalable — one whose yield source grows with adoption, not one borrowed from a centralized counterparty. Polaris is built to answer it.

## The lineage

Polaris is not starting from scratch. It descends from a line of immutable, counterparty-free CDP stablecoins:

![Polaris counterparty-free CDP lineage: SAI (2017), LUSD (2021), BOLD / Liquity V2 (2024), pUSD (2026)](/infographics/polaris-lineage.svg)

- **SAI (2017):** The first single-collateral CDP stablecoin. Proved the model works. Later became multi-collateral DAI, then drifted toward centralized backing — Real World Assets, USDC in the PSM, governance-controlled rates.
- **LUSD (2021):** Immutable, counterparty-free, governance-free. Years with no admin breach. But capital-inefficient: it spent roughly 20% of its life more than 1% above peg, and its yield was limited to redemption fee revenue.
- **BOLD (2024):** Liquity V2 — refined CDP mechanics, multi-collateral, still trustless. A genuine improvement, still yield-constrained.
- **pUSD (2026):** The next chapter. A bonding-curve collateral that solves the overpeg problem and produces yield scaling with DeFi volume, without adding a counterparty.

The lineage's lesson: immutability and counterparty-freedom are achievable and proven — SAI, LUSD, and BOLD showed that. The missing piece has always been a scalable onchain yield source.

## The bonding curve: the engine of everything

The bonding curve is what makes Polaris different from every prior CDP stablecoin.

When you deposit ETH into Polaris, the curve issues you **pETH** at the current price. When you redeem pETH, you get ETH back — at the floor price (at minimum), or the market price (if higher).

### The floor price

The floor price is the minimum exchange rate between pETH and ETH under the bonding-curve invariants. It is designed to rise as swap or conversion fees accumulate and a portion adjusts the floor upward. No normal protocol action lowers it.

The implication: pETH is an ETH proxy with a contract-defined lower bound in ETH terms, not a fixed-price ETH substitute. Its market price can move against ETH, but the floor-to-market ratio frames the curve-implied drawdown before gas, slippage, MEV, smart-contract risk, and chain-liveness risk.

If pETH's market price is 0.95 ETH and its floor is 0.85 ETH, your worst-case redemption is 0.85 ETH. As fees accumulate, that floor may rise to 0.87, then 0.90. It never falls.

### Onchain-only yield

The curve earns fees on every ETH↔pETH swap. Those fees raise the floor. So pETH's yield comes from trading volume — from real onchain activity — not from a T-bill desk or a CEX lending book.

The key insight: **the more DeFi activity flows through Polaris, the higher the floor rises, and the more valuable pETH becomes.** Yield scales with adoption instead of being capped by whatever a counterparty can deliver.

## pAssets: a factory of synthetic assets

With pETH as the collateral primitive, Polaris runs a factory of CDP (Collateralized Debt Position) instances. Each issues a different synthetic asset against the same collateral:

- **pUSD** — pegged to the US dollar
- **pGOLD** — pegged to gold
- **pCHF** — pegged to the Swiss franc
- (additional pAssets are deployable as new CDP instances)

### How a CDP works

You lock pETH in a **trove** and mint a pAsset against it. Your collateral ratio must stay above the minimum threshold for your trove type. If it falls below, your trove is liquidated — the Stability Pool absorbs the debt and takes your collateral at a discount.

The peg is maintained by two opposing arbitrage forces:

**Below peg:** Anyone can redeem pAssets for pETH at face value (minus a small fee). If the system is under-backed, redemptions are scaled down in proportion to the reserve backing. This burns pAssets, reduces supply, and drives the price back up.

**Above peg:** Anyone can open a trove, mint pAssets cheaply, and sell them above peg for a profit. This increases supply and drives the price back down.

### Autonomous interest rates

Polaris sets borrower interest rates without an external price oracle. It uses redemption and minting volume as proxy signals:

- High redemption volume → the pAsset is trading below peg → rates rise to deter new minting.
- Low redemption volume → the peg is healthy → rates fall to encourage minting.

This makes rate-setting self-contained and tamper-proof.

### The Stability Pool

The Stability Pool is the safety net for every CDP. Holders deposit pAssets; when a trove is liquidated, the pool absorbs the debt and takes the collateral at a discount. Depositors earn:

1. A share of borrower interest from all CDPs.
2. Discounted pETH from liquidations.
3. Any additional protocol incentives, if enabled, are expected to come from routed hard-asset revenue rather than POLAR emissions.

It is the primary yield venue for pAsset holders and the mechanism that keeps the CDP system solvent.

## POLAR: stewardship through alignment

POLAR is the protocol's stewardship token. Apart from a one-time genesis allocation of 100,000,000 POLAR minted to the stewardship address at deployment, its issuance is deliberately constrained: **beyond that fixed initial supply, POLAR can only be minted by burning pETH in a conversion auction.** There is no other ongoing issuance path — no investor unlock, no inflationary mint function.

### What burning pETH in a conversion auction does

When pETH is burned:
1. The ETH backing the burned pETH is freed from the bonding curve's floor reserve — a configurable rebate goes to the converter and the remainder is recycled into the system (market-buying pETH to refill the reward buffer).
2. The pETH floor price rises (supply contraction).
3. POLAR is minted to the burner.

All three effects are positive for the system. Burning pETH is the highest-alignment action a user can take.


### vePOLAR and stewardship

A designated stewardship role can steer quantitative parameters — fee and interest-rate settings, the conversion parameters — within the bounds the immutable contracts define. It cannot modify the bonding curve logic, CDP mechanics, liquidation rules, or the fixed collateralization thresholds.

The distinction matters: Polaris has stewardship, not governance. The core is fixed; only the dials within it are adjustable.

## The system end to end

![Polaris system: ETH enters the bonding curve as pETH, pETH collateralizes CDPs that mint pAssets, burning pETH for POLAR raises the floor and releases ETH](/polaris-system-v2.svg)

The four-step picture:
1. **Swap.** ETH enters the bonding curve and is issued as pETH at the current price.
2. **Collateralize.** pETH is the only collateral every CDP instance accepts.
3. **Borrow.** A CDP mints a pAsset — pUSD, pGOLD, or any other — against that pETH.
4. **Stewardship.** Burning pETH for POLAR raises the floor, releases ETH, and aligns token holders with the protocol's long-term health.

## The flywheel

![Polaris flywheel: more adoption drives activity, activity generates fees, fees fund conversions, conversions raise the pETH floor, a higher floor strengthens collateral, stronger collateral enables more minting, which drives more adoption](/infographics/polaris-flywheel.svg)

> Adoption → activity → fees → conversions → higher pETH floor → stronger collateral → more minting → more adoption.

Each component reinforces the others:

- More pAsset CDPs create demand for pETH.
- Demand for pETH generates bonding curve swap fees.
- Fees raise the pETH floor.
- A higher floor makes every existing CDP position safer (the collateral is worth more in ETH terms).
- Safer positions lower the protocol's risk profile, attracting more users.
- More users generate more activity, and the loop continues.

This compounding is structural, not dependent on token emissions.

## Three intended invariants

| Invariant | How it is enforced |
|---|---|
| **pETH floor is designed to rise** | Bonding curve math — no normal protocol action in the intended implementation lowers it |
| **No counterparty in the collateral stack** | pETH is backed by ETH; pAssets are backed by pETH; nothing else enters |
| **Immutable core** | Bonding curve, CDP logic, liquidations, and redemptions are fixed in bytecode; there is no admin key and no upgrade function for the core |

These are not marketing promises. They are intended invariants that must be verified against the audited production contracts once addresses and reports are published.

## Risk model

Understanding Polaris means knowing what can and cannot happen:

**What can happen:**
- Your trove can be liquidated if your collateral ratio falls below the minimum. You keep any surplus above the liquidation threshold — liquidations are not a total loss.
- Recovery Mode triggers when the protocol's reserve backing relative to total debt (its reserve-to-debt ratio) falls below a threshold. While active, borrowing tightens — actions that increase debt or reduce collateral must meet a higher minimum collateral ratio and may not worsen a position's ratio — but liquidation eligibility stays at the normal minimum collateral ratio, and pure collateral top-ups and repayments remain allowed.
- pETH's market price can fall against ETH and with ETH. The floor defines a curve-level lower-bound quote under stated assumptions; it does not protect against ETH falling against other units.

**What cannot happen by design, once the intended production contracts are verified:**
- No normal protocol action can lower the pETH floor under the intended curve invariant.
- The CDP or bonding curve logic cannot be upgraded.
- Your pAsset balance cannot be frozen or confiscated.
