# Polaris Liquidations


<Callout variant="danger" title="Before You Act">
Borrowers: do not manage a trove from the liquidation line. Check the app's current minimum ICR, Recovery Mode status, accrued interest, collateral-price freshness, and gas conditions before withdrawing collateral or minting more debt. Stability Pool depositors: liquidations convert part of your pAsset deposit into pETH exposure.
</Callout>

## What a liquidation is

A **liquidation** closes a trove that has fallen below its **minimum collateralization ratio** — the point at which its collateral no longer comfortably covers its debt. The protocol cancels the trove's debt and seizes its pETH collateral.

The purpose is solvency. Every pAsset is a claim on collateral; leaving undercollateralized troves open would leave the pAsset not fully backed. Liquidations are how Polaris is designed to keep backing intact, subject to functioning contracts, live Ethereum execution, available gas, valid pricing data, keeper/executor availability, and audited invariants.

Unlike a [redemption](/redemptions-liquidations), a liquidation is not an arbitrage you can opt into at par — it is a forced close triggered by your own ratio crossing a hard line.

## Where the debt and collateral go

Liquidation runs in two tiers.

1. **The Stability Pool absorbs it first.** The [Stability Pool](/yield/stability-pool) holds a reserve of the pAsset. When a trove is liquidated, the pool burns pAsset equal to the trove's debt and receives its pETH collateral in return. Because liquidated troves are seized while still *overcollateralized* (above 100%, below the minimum), the pool typically receives more value in pETH than the pAsset it burns. That surplus is the depositors' compensation.

2. **Redistribution is the fallback.** If the Stability Pool is empty or too small to cover the debt, the trove's debt *and* collateral spread across the remaining troves on that pAsset, in proportion to their size. Other borrowers take on a slice of debt and a matching slice of pETH. The system stays solvent either way.

| Path | Trigger | Absorbs debt | Receives collateral |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stability Pool** | Pool has sufficient pAsset | Pool burns its pAsset | Depositors, pro-rata |
| **Redistribution** | Pool empty / insufficient | Other troves take it on | Other troves, pro-rata |

**Collateral surplus.** When the Stability Pool absorbs a liquidation, the trove surrenders only what covers its debt, a liquidation penalty, and gas compensation for the liquidator. Any collateral beyond that goes to the **Collateral Surplus Pool**, where the former borrower can claim it. (Collateral spread through the redistribution fallback is not returned this way.)

## Worked example: liquidation

The numbers below are illustrative only. Suppose the app shows a 120% minimum ICR for a pUSD trove. A borrower has 10,000 pUSD of debt and pETH collateral valued at \$11,800, so the ICR is 118% — below the illustrative minimum, and liquidatable.

If the Stability Pool is large enough, it burns 10,000 pUSD and receives the trove's pETH. The borrower's debt is cleared; the collateral covers the debt, the liquidation penalty, and gas compensation, with any surplus returned for the borrower to claim. Depositors receive pETH pro rata; in a normal overcollateralized liquidation, that pETH should be worth more than the pUSD burned, but the exact gain depends on the final penalty, execution timing, pETH price, gas, and slippage.

If the Stability Pool is empty or too small, the uncovered portion follows the redistribution path: remaining troves inherit a proportional slice of both the liquidated debt and collateral.

## Risks

<Callout variant="danger" title="Warning — For Borrowers">
Liquidation is involuntary and can happen fast. A liquidated trove gives up its collateral to cover the debt plus a liquidation penalty and gas compensation; any surplus is returned to you to claim, but the penalty makes liquidation a real loss to avoid. Keep a <strong>buffer</strong> above the minimum ratio so a sharp move in collateral price does not cross the line. The thinner your margin, the less room you have when volatility hits. See <a href="/minting/managing-your-trove">Managing Your Trove</a>.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="danger" title="Warning — For Stability Pool Depositors">
You are the system's first-loss backstop. In a flash crash, troves can be liquidated near — or in extreme cases below — 100% collateralization, and you may receive pETH worth less than the pAsset burned. Over a full cycle the surplus from healthy liquidations compensates for that risk, but it is not riskless yield. See <a href="/yield/stability-pool">The Stability Pool</a> and the <a href="/resources/risk-disclosure">Risk Disclosure</a>.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="info">
Exact liquidation thresholds, penalties, and the minimum collateralization ratio per pAsset are finalized closer to launch.
</Callout>

## How liquidation interacts with system health

A trove's individual ratio is not all that matters. When system-wide backing runs thin, the protocol enters [Recovery Mode](/redemptions-liquidations/recovery-mode), which tightens risk-increasing borrower operations while it lasts. Liquidation eligibility is unchanged: a trove is liquidated whenever its ICR falls below the minimum, in every mode. Recovery Mode is the system-level safeguard complementing the trove-level liquidation described here.

Liquidations are not punishment — they are the mechanism designed to keep a pAsset backed by the collateral it claims. Keep a buffer, and you are less likely to lose your trove.
