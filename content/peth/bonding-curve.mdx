# pETH Bonding Curve

The bonding curve is the foundation of Polaris. From this one primitive come pETH, its rising floor, [Split](/peth/split), reserve loans, and value capture on every interaction.

## What is a bonding curve?

A two-way bonding curve lets you swap like a regular AMM pool, with one key difference: **it needs no liquidity providers.** Instead, it enforces a mathematical relationship between a token's price and its supply:

- Demand rises, tokens are minted, price increases.
- Tokens are burned/sold, price decreases.

Buying adds ETH to the reserve and **mints** pETH. Selling withdraws from the reserve and **burns** pETH. The result is contract-native liquidity with no external liquidity provider, subject to Ethereum liveness, available gas, MEV, slippage, and the bonding-curve contracts working as designed. You have used bonding curves without knowing it: every AMM is one (Uniswap, Curve), and Pump.fun is a well-known example.

Two things make pETH's curve different from Pump.fun's:

1. **Purpose**: Pump.fun raises capital to seed liquidity; Polaris uses the curve to *back* pETH permanently.
2. **Temporality**: Pump.fun's curve terminates at "graduation"; the pETH curve is designed to keep running.

## What's unique about pETH's curve

pETH's curve is **two-way** (ETH ⇒ pETH and pETH ⇒ ETH) and enforces a price floor for pETH, measured in ETH, under the curve's audited invariants. Two mechanisms are designed to lift the floor, while normal protocol actions are not designed to lower it: swap fees, charged in pETH and periodically burned, and [conversion auctions](/polar/conversion-auctions) that burn pETH to mint POLAR. See [The Floor Price](/peth/floor-price) for the assumptions behind that floor.

![pETH bonding curve: ETH deposits mint pETH along a power-law curve with a rising floor price](/infographics/polaris-bonding-curve.svg)

A rising floor is more than a safety hatch for holders. It lets the **whole protocol capture value on each interaction**, and defines a reserve-backed exit path while the contracts are live, Ethereum is processing transactions, and users can pay execution costs.

### The maths

The curve follows the formula:

$$
P = \alpha \cdot Q^{\beta}
$$

- **α**: a scaling parameter of little importance.
- **Q**: the supply of pETH.
- **β**: the power-law exponent that sets the curve's behaviour.

As supply `Q` increases, the price of pETH (in ETH) increases. **β** sets how tightly pETH tracks ETH: a large β decouples pETH and makes it volatile; a small β couples them closely. Polaris will use **β around 0.3**: close coupling, with room for interesting dynamics.

> *The exact final β is disclosed closer to launch.*

## pETH beyond the CDP

pETH is tailor-made collateral for Polaris CDPs, but it has a life of its own.

### Reserve loans and Split

You can [split pETH](/peth/split) into **fpETH** and **vpETH**. fpETH is the floor-price leg, and it can be used in **reserve loans** to borrow WETH from the reserve against floor-value collateral. These loans are:

- **Not liquidated by pETH premium swings**: borrow sizing is based on the floor-price leg, not the market premium.
- **Withdrawable through repayment**: repay the WETH owed and reclaim your fpETH while the contracts and Ethereum are live.
- **Recorded onchain as an NFT.**

Because the loan is sized to the floor price, it is designed to stay solvent for the protocol under the floor-price invariant: a borrower who never repays has effectively sold the fpETH floor claim to the reserve. The position can still accrue debt and depends on repayment, approvals, gas, contract correctness, and live Ethereum execution.

### DeFi composability

pETH's rising floor and eventual deep ETH backing could make it a prime asset *outside* Polaris too. A pETH/ETH market on Aave, Morpho, or Euler is a natural fit, much like stETH across DeFi today.

## pETH vs stETH

stETH is a prime asset, but it lacks contract-native liquidity from its own reserve. Native withdrawals depend on the validator exit queue and can vary; DEX liquidity is deep, but depends on liquidity providers who can stop supplying in a panic.

| Feature | **pETH** | **stETH** |
|---|---|---|
| Liquidity | Bonding-curve reserve, subject to contract and chain liveness | Deep, but LP-dependent |
| Withdrawal | Onchain sell path, with gas/slippage/MEV | Validator-exit queue; timing varies |
| Price floor | Contract-enforced under audited invariants | None |
| Main risk | Price risk vs ETH, bounded by the floor under the curve assumptions | LP/liquidity risk |

> stETH is a prime asset, don't get us wrong. It just doesn't provide this kind of reserve-backed onchain sell path.

## Risk overview

pETH carries three main risks:

1. **Technical risk**: the bonding curve is a smart contract; technical risk is unavoidable, mitigated through audits and modeling.
2. **Execution risk**: using the curve still requires Ethereum liveness, available gas, and transactions that can tolerate slippage and MEV.
3. **Price risk**: pETH is volatile against ETH. Your exposure depends on your entry price relative to the floor, i.e. the [floor ratio](/peth/floor-price): a higher ratio means a smaller curve-implied drawdown before execution costs.

The curve design removes liquidity-provider withdrawal risk. That does not make execution riskless: the curve provides a contract-defined buy/sell path and floor while the relevant invariants hold.
